History

Structural correspondence survey with the five-stage model (Ba, Nami, En, Uzu, Taba)

Survey Overview

  • Scope: 10 major theories in history
  • Research question: Do theories in history structurally correspond to the five-stage model?
  • Results: Strong correspondence 4, Partial correspondence 5, Conditional correspondence 1

Overview of the Five-Stage Model

StageDefinition
Ba (Field)An undifferentiated state. Initial conditions where neither direction nor structure has yet been determined
Nami (Wave)A stage of exploration where multiple directions diverge and compete
En (Edge)A state of tension where opposing elements coexist without converging to either side. A place where elements meet at boundaries, influence each other, and relationships emerge
Uzu (Vortex)A stage where new coherence (order) spontaneously arises from within tension
Taba (Bundle)A stage where form is established and stabilizes as a reusable structure

Key Entry 1: Path Dependence and Critical Junctures (Pierson, Mahoney, North)

  • Path dependence is the idea that initial choices lock in an institutional trajectory through positive feedback, creating self-reinforcing and long-lasting effects. Pierson (2000) identified four self-reinforcing mechanisms: sunk cost effects (resources already invested make withdrawal difficult), learning effects (continued use increases proficiency), coordination effects (benefits increase as more actors adopt the same institution), and adaptive expectations (adoption accelerates when the institution is expected to persist).
  • A critical juncture refers to a moment when institutional flexibility temporarily increases and multiple pathways open up. The choices made at this moment determine the long-term institutional direction.
  • As fact: Pierson (2000) and Mahoney (2000), within the framework of historical institutionalism, described how initial choices lock in institutional trajectories through positive feedback, and how multiple pathways temporarily open at critical junctures. North (1990) analyzed the mechanisms by which institutional change determines economic performance.
  • As reading: Here we read a sequential process of institutional branching, lock-in, and re-branching through crisis. The level of similarity is process. In particular, the critical juncture -- a "moment when multiple pathways compete and none is yet determined" -- most clearly manifests as a branching-point structure. Additionally, lock-in -- "irreversible pathway fixation through self-reinforcement" -- stands out as a persistence mechanism.
  • As interpretation: A state where multiple institutional options coexist corresponds to Ba. A phase where selection pressure rises due to crisis or political upheaval corresponds to Nami. The critical juncture -- where coalitions of actors and institutional proposals compete at a branching point that has not yet resolved -- structurally corresponds to En in the five-stage model. The process of positive feedback activating self-reinforcement after selection corresponds to Uzu. The stage where legitimizing norms and practical routines accumulate to establish lock-in corresponds to Taba. It is also characteristic that re-branching through institutional crisis (a return from Taba to Ba) is built into the theory.

Key Entry 2: Ibn Khaldun's Asabiyyah and Dynastic Theory (1377)

  • Ibn Khaldun was a 14th-century North African thinker who, in his Muqaddimah (Prolegomena), described the mechanism by which group cohesion (asabiyyah) drives the rise and fall of dynasties. His structural historical analysis, predating modern social science by several centuries, has been re-evaluated since the late 20th century.
  • As fact: Ibn Khaldun described how asabiyyah (group cohesion) surpasses mere leadership to achieve royal authority; how dynasties follow a cycle of cohesion, acquisition of power, expansion, luxurious decline, erosion of cohesion, and replacement (roughly 3-4 generations, about 120 years); and how immersion in luxury and prosperity structurally weakens cohesion. He also noted that after replacement, institutions (taxation, governance practices) persist and are reused by succeeding dynasties.
  • As reading: Here we read a cyclical process of cohesion generation, integration, expansion, erosion, and replacement. The level of similarity encompasses both process and structure. Two points are particularly noteworthy. First, the mechanism of "multiple cohesions merging into a larger cohesion" as a form of relationship formation. Second, the paradoxical collapse mechanism where "luxury erodes cohesion" -- where success weakens the system.
  • As interpretation: The social space where cohesions are scattered corresponds to Ba. The phase where one cohesion seeks dominance and competition intensifies corresponds to Nami. The integration of cohesion -- relationship networks formed through subordination, annexation, and alliance -- corresponds to En, and this correspondence is assessed as high. Dynastic expansion and institutional self-reinforcement correspond to Uzu. Replacement through cohesion erosion and institutional persistence correspond to the return from Taba to Ba. However, because the previous dynasty's institutions are reused by the next, this is not a complete return to an initial state but rather a spiral return where the legacy of one cycle shapes the initial conditions of the next.

Key Entry 3: Hughes' Large Technological Systems and Momentum (1983, 1987)

  • Hughes presented the history of large technological systems such as electrical grids, telephone networks, and railways as phases of "invention, development, innovation, transfer, and growth/competition/consolidation." He explicitly noted that these phases are not simply sequential but can overlap and reverse. His representative work Networks of Power (1983) examined the comparative history of electrification (United States, Germany, Britain) and is widely referenced as a foundational work in Science and Technology Studies (STS).
  • As fact: Hughes (1987) stated that as technological systems mature, they acquire not "autonomy" but "momentum." Momentum is the state in which organizations, people, fixed assets, and sunk costs are bundled together and projected into the future. He also noted that the actors involved in system building shift from inventor types during the invention phase to managerial types during the growth phase and to financial/political actors during the consolidation phase.
  • As reading: Here we read a process where technological and social elements are joined, self-propagate, and persist as inertia. The level of similarity is structure. The concept of momentum is particularly noteworthy. The description of "persistence of structure with direction" speaks the definition of the five-stage model's Taba almost verbatim in the vocabulary of technology history. Additionally, the acknowledgment of phase overlap and reversal is consistent with the five-stage model's "non-strictly unidirectional sequence."
  • As interpretation: The undifferentiated state of scattered technological and institutional elements corresponds to Ba. The emergence of radical invention corresponds to Nami. Development, transfer, and adaptation -- the joining of technological elements with social elements (law, markets, organizations) -- correspond to En. Growth, competition, and consolidation through system self-propagation and expansion correspond to Uzu. Momentum -- persistence as a bundle of fixed assets, organizations, regulations, and human resources -- corresponds to Taba. The correspondence across all five stages is high, making this the case that most naturally aligns with the five stages among the ten theories.

Cross-Cutting Patterns

  • The most prominent feature across history is that En is described through diverse mechanisms
  • This polymorphism means that when reading the five-stage model's En as "the phase where order is reorganized within relationships," history provides the richest set of examples
  • Ibn Khaldun (erosion of cohesion through luxury), Turchin (elite overproduction), and Vico (return to barbarism through civilizational corruption) independently describe the pattern of "success destabilizing the system"
  • Among the 10 theories of history, at least 5 (Ibn Khaldun, Vico, Turchin, path dependence, Wallerstein) have the return from Taba to Ba built into their theories

Unresolved Questions

  • How to reconcile cyclical models in history (Ibn Khaldun, Vico, Turchin) with the five-stage model has not yet been settled. While the five stages describe sequential unfolding, cyclical models describe repetition. The relationship between the two can be read as "one cycle of repetition corresponds to one round of the five stages," but discretion remains in how to delineate cycles.
  • How to understand "Ba" in history requires continued examination. Whether to maintain the understanding of an undifferentiated initial state, or to reread it as preconditions that already carry orientation -- as in Koselleck's "space of experience" or Braudel's "long duration" -- changes the scope of the model.
  • Momentum (Hughes) and lock-in (path dependence) may describe different aspects of Taba. Momentum is "inertia with direction" and lock-in is "pathway fixation." The relationship between the two may offer clues for understanding the internal structure of Taba.
  • The weak correspondence with White (Metahistory) inversely illuminates the scope of the five-stage model. Whether the five stages describe "the creation of existence" and "the generation of understanding" (the structuring of historical narrative) lies outside this scope, or whether the scope needs to be expanded, remains as a methodological challenge.

Conclusion

  • This survey confirmed that history is a domain with moderate to high structural similarity to the five-stage model overall
  • Particularly strong correspondence was shown by Hughes (Large Technological Systems), path dependence, Ibn Khaldun (Asabiyyah and Dynastic Theory), and Turchin (Cliodynamics)
  • Two major insights emerge from history
