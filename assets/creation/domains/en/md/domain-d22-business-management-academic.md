# Domain Commentary D22: Business & Management

## 0. How to Read This

This commentary analyzes whether existing management theories and the five-stage creation model (Field → Wave → Relation → Vortex → Bundle) point to the same structural patterns under different labels. The analysis does not argue that existing models are insufficient. It maps where labels overlap, where they diverge, and what each framework reveals that the others do not.

Start with the comparison table (Section 4) for an overview, then Section 5 for points of divergence.

## 1. What Was Done

- **Domain**: D22 Business & Management
- **Purpose**: Structural comparison between the five-stage model and established management theories
- **Method**: Theories were compared not by whether they have the same number of stages, but by what changes at each transition—the meaning of progression, not its form. Points of alignment and misalignment were recorded simultaneously.

## 2. Theories Compared

Five theories were selected for breadth and comparability:

- **Tuckman** (Team development stages) — primary focus
- **Nonaka & Takeuchi** (SECI model / Ba)
- **March** (Exploration / Exploitation)
- **Weick** (Sensemaking)
- **Christensen** (Disruptive innovation)

Tuckman was adopted as the primary comparison point because its stage descriptions are explicit, making the structural comparison most transparent for readers unfamiliar with the five-stage model.

## 3. Analytical Procedure

1. Summarize each theory's core claims at equivalent granularity
2. Summarize the five-stage model at the same granularity
3. Compare by transition meaning (what changes), not by stage count
4. Record misalignments and boundary conditions alongside alignments
5. Assign evidence strength (high/medium/low) based on literature specificity and falsifiability

## 5. Points of Divergence and Falsification Candidates

**Tuckman**: If team development frequently exhibits stage reordering (e.g., Norming before Storming), the fixed correspondence with the five-stage model weakens. This is an empirically testable prediction.

**SECI**: If cases exist where outcomes emerge without conceptualization or justification phases, the correspondence is limited to specific knowledge-creation contexts rather than being structural.

**Disruptive Innovation**: If the theory's predictive failures accumulate (cases it cannot explain post-hoc), the structural comparison remains descriptive rather than explanatory.

**Cross-scale validity**: Tuckman operates at team scale; Christensen at industry scale. Whether both can be legitimately compared against the same five-stage framework requires further examination. This is a methodological limitation of the current analysis.

## 6. What This Reveals

**Similarity**: Tuckman's Norming stage and the five-stage Relation share a structural function—the stage where contradictions are held rather than resolved. Both describe a period that is uncomfortable and frequently bypassed, but determines the quality of what follows.

**Distinctiveness**: The five-stage model names the Relation stage explicitly and proposes it as the discriminating structural moment. In Tuckman, Norming is described primarily as norm establishment; the five-stage model foregrounds the contradiction-holding function.

**Learning**: Nonaka's Ba concept highlights that generative preconditions can be intentionally designed at organizational level—a dimension the five-stage model currently addresses only at individual level (M2), with team extension (M3) as future work.

**Context**: Management theories operate at organizational and market scales. The five-stage model's hypothesis that the same structure applies from quantum to cosmic scale means these correspondences, if real, would be instances of a more general pattern. Whether this generality holds is an open question.

## 7. Limitations and Next Steps

- This commentary does not evaluate all D22 theories at uniform depth
- Next: specify falsifiable conditions for each theory ("under what conditions does the correspondence break?")
- Reassess the validity of comparing team-scale theories (Tuckman) and industry-scale theories (Christensen) on the same structural axis
