# Order Formation in Chemistry and Its Structural Correspondence with the Five-Stage Model

![Order Formation in Chemistry and Its Structural Correspondence with the Five-Stage Model — Structural correspondence diagram](https://uminomae.github.io/pjdhiro/assets/creation/img/svg/domains/en/domain-D03-chemistry.svg)

## 1. Purpose and Question of the Investigation

![Order Formation in Chemistry and Its Structural Correspondence with the Five-Stage Model — 調査概要インフォグラフィック](https://uminomae.github.io/pjdhiro/assets/creation/img/svg/domains/ja/D03-01-overview-svg.svg)


This report summarizes an investigation into whether major theories of order formation in chemistry correspond structurally to the five-stage model (Field -> Wave -> Edge -> Vortex -> Bundle).

The five-stage model is a theoretical framework that describes creative processes through five stages: Field, an undifferentiated initial condition in which neither direction nor structure is fixed; Wave, a stage of divergent and competing exploration; Edge, a tense condition in which opposing elements coexist without collapsing into either side; Vortex, the spontaneous emergence of a new coherence from within that tension; and Bundle, the stabilization of that coherence as a reusable structure. This investigation examines how far that framework can be mapped onto the discipline of chemistry.

**What this report covers**: For ten chemical theories and phenomena, it reports whether structural correspondence with the five-stage model can be observed, and if so, how strong that correspondence appears to be.

**What this report does not cover**: The aim is not to prove any given theory correct, nor to provide a comprehensive textbook-style survey of chemistry. Even when structural correspondence is observed, that is an observation of similarity in structure, not a claim of identity.

## 2. Method of Investigation

### 2.1 Selection of Cases

Ten theories and phenomena were selected from chemistry, all related to order formation, phase transition, or self-organization. The selection criteria were: (1) the theory or phenomenon is established within chemistry or grounded in reproducible experimental findings; (2) it describes some process by which order is generated; and (3) it is detailed enough to allow examination of possible correspondence with the five-stage model.

### 2.2 Method of Evaluation

For each theory, structural correspondence was examined from several independent angles. Concretely, this meant: (1) identifying the core mechanism of the theory; (2) examining whether structures corresponding to each of the five stages are present; and (3) giving particular weight to whether there is a clear analogue of the Edge stage, that is, a tense configuration in which opposing factors coexist.

### 2.3 Criteria of Judgment

The strength of correspondence was judged by the following criteria.

- **Strong structural correspondence**: Clear counterparts exist for each of the five stages, and the mechanism of transition between stages also corresponds.
- **Partial correspondence**: Several stages correspond, but one or more stages remain unclear.
- **Conditional correspondence**: Correspondence holds only under specific conditions, or the interpretation remains open to debate.

### 2.4 Limits of the Investigation

This investigation has several limits. (1) It does not cover all of chemistry; it is restricted to theories related to the generation of order. (2) Judgments about structural correspondence inevitably involve interpretation, so different evaluators could reasonably differ. (3) There is a difference in precision between the quantitative descriptions used in chemistry and the qualitative descriptions used in the five-stage model.

## 3. Model Overview: What the Five-Stage Model Is

The five-stage model is a framework for describing structures common to creative processes. It consists of the following five stages.

| Stage | Name | Definition |
|------|------|------|
| Stage 1 | Field | An undifferentiated condition in which neither direction nor structure has yet been fixed |
| Stage 2 | Wave | A stage of exploration in which multiple directions diverge and compete |
| Stage 3 | Edge | A tense condition in which opposing elements coexist without converging into either side |
| Stage 4 | Vortex | A stage in which a new coherence or order rises spontaneously out of the tension |
| Stage 5 | Bundle | A stage in which the form is fixed and stabilized as a reusable structure |

This model is not limited to one scale or one discipline. It has been proposed as a general framework for describing how order comes into being, from molecular-scale chemical reactions to changes in ecosystems.

Some readers may already feel discomfort or skepticism about the definitions given so far. That reaction is natural, and is itself relevant to the investigation. The point of the following sections is to make the explanatory power of the framework concrete by testing it against specific findings.

### 4.2 Overall Tendencies

Chemistry is a domain in which structures corresponding to Edge can often be described quantitatively as physical entities: critical nuclei, interfaces, bifurcation points, and reaction-diffusion fronts. In five of the ten cases, the clarity of Edge was rated high. That is a particularly high level among the thirty domains investigated.

In particular, the BZ reaction and Turing patterns in reaction-diffusion systems belong among the strongest examples in the whole investigation, because all five stages can be observed experimentally and described with mathematical rigor.

## 5. Results: Findings by Theory

### 5.1 The BZ Reaction and Dissipative Structures: Non-Equilibrium as the Condition of Order

The BZ reaction, the Belousov-Zhabotinsky reaction, is a chemical oscillation in which the color of a solution changes periodically. Belousov observed the phenomenon around 1951 and published a brief report in 1959, but it was rejected by major journals on the grounds that chemical oscillation appeared to contradict the second law of thermodynamics. It was only after Zhabotinsky's systematic studies in the late 1960s that the phenomenon gained wide international recognition.

Field-Koros-Noyes (1972) clarified the chemical mechanism and proposed a reduced model later called the Oregonator (Field-Noyes 1974). In that model, positive feedback by autocatalysis combines with delayed negative feedback, destabilizing the steady state and generating oscillation. In space, spiral waves and target patterns appear (Winfree 1972).

Prigogine's theory of dissipative structures, recognized by the 1977 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, provided the broader theoretical frame. In an open system far from equilibrium, macroscopic order forms spontaneously while entropy is still being produced. That is what is meant by a dissipative structure.

**Correspondence with the five stages**: A uniform reactive solution corresponds to Field. Hopf bifurcation produced by competition between positive and negative feedback loops corresponds to Wave. The neighborhood of the bifurcation point, the critical moment at which the system's fate is decided by minute fluctuations, corresponds to Edge. Self-sustaining rotating order in the form of limit cycles or spiral waves corresponds to Vortex. A stabilized spatiotemporal pattern corresponds to Bundle.

What is particularly notable here is that the bifurcation point exhibits all three defining features of Edge. One can identify nonlinear interaction between activators and inhibitors as a relational network, the role of microscopic fluctuation in determining the direction of branching as indeterminacy, and the direct connection from bifurcation to dissipative structure as a transition into Vortex.

Prigogine's insight that non-equilibrium is the condition of order corresponds closely to the five-stage model's assumption that fluctuation, and therefore non-equilibrium, is the point of departure for order formation.

### 5.2 Reaction-Diffusion Turing Patterns: Inhibition Makes Creation Possible

Turing (1952) showed mathematically that a system composed of an activator and an inhibitor with different diffusion coefficients can spontaneously generate spatial patterns, such as spots and stripes, from an initially uniform state. Ordinarily diffusion should homogenize matter and stabilize the system, yet when the inhibitor diffuses faster than the activator, diffusion itself drives instability. The result is counterintuitive.

Meinhardt and Gierer (1972, 2000) generalized this as local self-activation and long-range inhibition, positioning it as a basic principle of morphogenesis. Experimental confirmation was later achieved in settings such as the CIMA reaction (Castets et al. 1990) and research on zebrafish stripe formation (Kondo and Miura 2010).

**Correspondence with the five stages**: A uniform concentration field corresponds to Field. The process in which small fluctuations are amplified exponentially by reaction-diffusion instability and a particular wave number is selected corresponds to Wave. The spatial boundary formed by activator and inhibitor corresponds to Edge. The self-organization of discrete patterns such as spots and stripes corresponds to Vortex. The stabilized state in which a particular pattern is selected and maintained corresponds to Bundle.

The significance of this theory for the five-stage model lies in the fact that it demonstrates mathematically that constraint, in the form of inhibition, makes creation possible. Without the long-range diffusion of the inhibitor, the uniform state would remain stable and no pattern would appear. The same equations also remain valid across scales, from micrometer-scale reaction lengths to kilometer-scale vegetation patterns, which strengthens confidence in the structural correspondence.

### 5.3 Crystal Nucleation: A Quantitative Description of Edge as the Critical Nucleus

In a supersaturated solution, a new phase such as a crystal does not immediately grow to macroscopic size. First, tiny clusters or nuclei form. Growth begins only when a cluster exceeds a critical size. Clusters smaller than that disappear. The branching here is governed by the opposition between the volume-energy gain from crystallization, which becomes more favorable as the cluster grows and scales with r^3, and the cost of interface energy, which scales with surface area and therefore with r^2. Classical nucleation theory, originating in Gibbs (1878) and refined by Becker-Doring (1935) and Turnbull-Fisher (1949), describes this process.

More recently, Gebauer et al. (2018) and others have reported non-classical nucleation pathways involving amorphous precursors or prenucleation clusters. These findings suggest that the critical nucleus may function not as a mathematical point but as a region traversing several intermediate states.

**Correspondence with the five stages**: The metastable mother phase, supersaturated yet not crystallized, corresponds to Field. The appearance and disappearance of clusters due to thermal fluctuation corresponds to Wave. The critical nucleus, the boundary at which volume gain and interface cost balance and the fate of the cluster is decided, corresponds to Edge. The accelerated growth of a supercritical nucleus corresponds to Vortex. The stabilized macroscopic crystal corresponds to Bundle.

The critical nucleus is one of the clearest chemical realizations of Edge. The opposition between volume gain and interface cost is quantitatively describable, and the height of the free-energy barrier can be calculated. Non-classical pathways also suggest that Edge need not always be an instantaneous branching point; it may instead have width and function as a transitional band.

### 5.4 Block Copolymer Microphase Separation: Literal Waves Carry Order

A block copolymer is a molecule in which chemically different polymer chains, for example an A chain and a B chain, are linked by covalent bonds. If A and B are immiscible, one would normally expect macroscopic separation like oil and water. But because the chains are covalently connected, large-scale separation is prohibited. Instead, finely structured periodic morphologies such as lamellae, cylinders, and gyroids arise spontaneously on the nanoscale, typically around 5 to 100 nm. Leibler (1980) established the core theoretical framework for this phenomenon.

**Correspondence with the five stages**: The uniformly mixed state of A and B corresponds to Field. The appearance of unstable modes at a finite wave number q*, literally the selection of a specific wavelength, corresponds to Wave. The interface between A-rich and B-rich domains, necessarily produced by the tension between immiscibility and chain connectivity, corresponds to Edge. Domain coarsening and the rearrangement of defects correspond to Vortex. The stabilized microphase morphology and its periodicity correspond to Bundle.

What is particularly important in this case is that q*, the unstable mode at finite wave number, exists as a literal wave at the center of the theory. It is also a concrete example from polymer chemistry of the principle that relation in the form of constraint makes creation possible. Without covalent linkage, the system would simply undergo macroscopic separation and no delicate micro-order would emerge.

### 5.5 The Sol-Gel Transition: The Threshold of Connectedness

The transition from a sol, a fluid colloidal dispersion, to a gel, an elastic solid network, is the phenomenon in which a system-spanning network, an infinite cluster, suddenly appears as cross-linking proceeds. The foundational framework is given by the gelation theory of Flory (1941) and Stockmayer (1943), while percolation theory, especially as presented by Stauffer and Aharony (1994), describes the critical behavior near the gel point.

Winter and Chambon (1986) showed that at the gel point the storage modulus and the loss modulus increase with the same power law in frequency, meaning that tan delta becomes frequency-independent. This indicates that the system has a self-similar, fractal structure at the gel point.

**Correspondence with the five stages**: The sol state, in which monomers or oligomers are dispersed through the solution, corresponds to Field. The dynamic growth and merging of local clusters corresponds to Wave. The gel point, the threshold at which local bonds turn into global connectivity, corresponds to Edge. The sudden increase in gel fraction and the power-law growth of elasticity correspond to Vortex. The mature three-dimensional network corresponds to Bundle.

The gel point is one of the most intuitive expressions of Edge as a threshold of connectedness. The accumulation of local change, namely cross-linking, turns at a certain point into a global qualitative change, namely gelation. That matches the core Wave -> Edge -> Vortex sequence of the five-stage model. The emergence of fractal structure at the gel point is also a physical realization of the possibility that Edge may carry scale-invariant structure.

### 5.6 Other Theories: Summary and Main Points of Correspondence

**Catalytic reaction cycles (Michaelis-Menten / Langmuir-Hinshelwood)**: Enzymatic catalytic cycles such as E + S <-> ES -> E + P, and analogous surface-catalysis cycles, have a circular structure in which the catalyst is regenerated after product release. This is significant because it shows that the five stages need not occur only once; they can rotate. At the same time, the formation of the enzyme-substrate complex is determined by equilibrium constants, so the characteristic uncertainty of Edge, where fate genuinely wavers, is less visible. The correspondence is therefore partial.

**Supramolecular self-assembly (Lehn / Whitesides)**: Here higher-order structures form spontaneously from intermolecular non-covalent interactions without precise external machining. Lehn systematized this field as supramolecular chemistry and was recognized with the 1987 Nobel Prize. Reversibility, meaning the system can assemble while correcting errors, is the key feature. The structure that refusing immediate fixation makes arrival at the optimal structure possible corresponds closely to the five-stage model. Dynamic covalent chemistry (Lehn 2007), which uses reversible covalent bonds to build systems that correct mistakes as they assemble, strengthens the correspondence further.

**Branching explosion limits (the H2/O2 system)**: Hydrogen-oxygen mixtures exhibit three explosion limits, forming a complex boundary in temperature-pressure space between explosive and non-explosive regimes (Semenov 1935; Hinshelwood 1956, both Nobel-recognized work). The same system thus contains multiple qualitatively different kinds of Edge. This makes a distinctive contribution. Equally important, explosion is not order formation but destructive transformation. It shows that what lies beyond Edge is not always creation or order generation. Correspondence with Vortex -> Bundle holds only in a limited way in cases of steady combustion.

**The universal structure of chemical oscillation**: Chemical oscillation as seen in the BZ reaction is not unique to one reaction system. The Oregonator, the Brusselator, Lotka-Volterra dynamics, and the Goodwin oscillator all reduce to the same normal form near Hopf bifurcation. The two ingredients of autocatalysis and delayed negative feedback are described by the same mathematical structure across more than four orders of magnitude in scale, from molecular events measured in milliseconds to ecosystems evolving over years. That supports the idea that the five-stage model may capture a universality not tied to any one mechanism. The finding that Ca2+ oscillations induce different physiological responses at different frequencies (Berridge 1997) also suggests that Bundle need not mean only fixed material form; it can also mean informational fixation in a temporal pattern.

**Chemical evolution and the RNA world hypothesis**: The staged account inorganic -> organic -> self-replication -> metabolism -> cell could in principle correspond to the five stages. But because the boundaries between stages remain unclear and multiple competing scenarios remain in play, no specific structure corresponding to Edge can yet be identified with confidence. Judgment is therefore withheld for now.

## 6. Cross-Domain Patterns

![Order Formation in Chemistry and Its Structural Correspondence with the Five-Stage Model — 横断的パターン図](https://uminomae.github.io/pjdhiro/assets/creation/img/svg/domains/ja/D03-03-cross-patterns-svg.svg)


Across these individual theories, three recurring structural patterns appeared throughout the chemistry domain.

### 6.1 The Paradox That Constraint Makes Creation Possible

In Turing patterns, the long-range diffusion of the inhibitor makes pattern formation possible. In microphase separation, constraint through covalent linkage prevents macroscopic separation and generates delicate nanostructure. In supramolecular self-assembly, reversible bonding enables error correction and supports arrival at an optimal structure.

These three very different mechanisms all embody the same principle: without constraint or inhibition, order does not emerge. That finding corresponds structurally to the claim that Edge, the stage in which opposing forces coexist and easy discharge is blocked, is essential to the generation of order.

### 6.2 The Same Structure Holds in Both Equilibrium and Non-Equilibrium Systems

The BZ reaction, which produces dynamic order far from equilibrium, and supramolecular self-assembly, which converges thermodynamically in equilibrium conditions, differ fundamentally in mechanism. Yet both were judged to exhibit the structure Field -> Wave -> Edge -> Vortex -> Bundle. This suggests that the five-stage model may describe a more abstract structure not dependent on any single mechanism. At the same time, whether it is justified to speak of such different mechanisms within one common framework still requires further examination.

### 6.3 The Diversity of Edge

The forms of Edge observed in chemistry are not all the same. They include the critical nucleus, an energy-barrier type; the bifurcation point, a dynamical-systems type; the interface, a spatial-boundary type; the gel point, a percolation type; and explosion limits, a type generated by multiple competing mechanisms.

The branching explosion limits are especially important because they show that one and the same system can possess different kinds of Edge under different conditions. The first limit is produced by competition between wall reactions and gas-phase reactions. The second is produced by competition between chain branching and three-body termination. The third is produced by competition between heat generation and heat removal. This suggests the need for a more refined classification of Edge.

The explosion-limit case also shows that whether what lies beyond Edge becomes order formation or destructive transformation depends on the control conditions of the system. Clarifying the conditions under which the transition Edge -> Vortex -> Bundle holds should therefore be treated as a theoretical task for future work.

## 7. Unresolved Questions

The following questions remained unanswered, or still allow competing interpretations.

1. **How far the unification of equilibrium and non-equilibrium systems is justified**: To what extent is it theoretically defensible to describe dissipative structures under non-equilibrium conditions and supramolecular self-assembly in equilibrium conditions through the same five stages? Does this abstraction suppress too much of the mechanistic difference?

2. **A taxonomy of Edge**: Even within chemistry alone, several qualitatively distinct forms of Edge were confirmed. Is it possible to classify Edge according to the kinds of forces that are in opposition? Would a two-line classification, one centered on free-energy minimization in equilibrium systems and one centered on dynamical bifurcation in non-equilibrium systems, prove useful?

3. **Extending the concept of Bundle**: Ca2+ frequency coding in chemical oscillation suggests that Bundle may indicate not only material fixation but also informational fixation in temporal pattern. How far that concept should be extended remains unresolved.

4. **Limits of structural correspondence**: Chemistry allows quantitative description, which raises confidence in structural correspondence. But whether this same degree of precision can hold in other domains, especially in the humanities, cannot be decided from the chemistry investigation alone.

## 8. Conclusion

Chemistry is one of the domains in which structural correspondence with the five-stage model was confirmed especially strongly across the thirty domains studied. Some degree of correspondence was observed in nine of the ten cases investigated. The BZ reaction and Turing patterns stand among the highest-confidence examples in the entire investigation, because all five stages can be visualized experimentally and described with mathematical rigor.

In chemistry, Edge can be described quantitatively as a physical entity, in the form of a critical nucleus, a bifurcation point, an interface, or a gel point. This indicates that Edge in the five-stage model need not be a mere metaphor; it can correspond to observable physical structure.

At the same time, the following points remain hypotheses rather than settled facts.

- Whether the five-stage model is a universal structure independent of mechanism cannot be settled from chemistry alone. Cross-domain comparison is still needed.
- Confirmed structural correspondence does not mean that the five-stage model describes chemical phenomena correctly in any exhaustive sense. It means only that there is a similarity in descriptive structure between theories of order formation in chemistry and the five-stage model.
- Cases such as chemical evolution, where judgment had to be withheld, suggest that the model may have limits in its range of application.

The results in chemistry support, though conditionally, the structural validity of the five-stage model. The cross-cutting findings that constraint makes creation possible, that Edge has multiple qualitatively different types, and that the same structure can hold in both equilibrium and non-equilibrium systems provide useful clues for further refinement of the theory.
