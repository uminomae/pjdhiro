## 1. Purpose of the Investigation

This investigation addresses the following question:

> Do theories of developmental psychology correspond structurally to the five-stage model (Field, Wave, Edge, Vortex, Bundle)?

Developmental psychology is a discipline concerned with processes of change. Although "creation" is not its direct theme, the very concept of "development" describes the generation of new order and therefore naturally overlaps with the scope of the five-stage model. In particular, traditions that understand development not as the accumulation of knowledge but as qualitative transformation of structure were expected to show the most direct correspondence with the five-stage model.

The purpose of this report is to communicate the findings of the investigation accurately. It is neither a textbook of developmental psychology nor a guide to childrearing.

## 2. Method of Investigation

Eleven major theories in developmental psychology were selected for evaluation. They fall into the following five lineages:

- **Structural developmental theories**: Kegan (structural developmental theory), Cook-Greuter (ego development theory). A lineage that describes development as qualitative transformation of structure
- **Sociocultural approach**: Vygotsky (Zone of Proximal Development). A lineage that describes development as the internalization of social interaction
- **Dynamic systems approach**: Thelen & Smith. A lineage that describes development as self-organization and phase transition
- **Classical stage theories**: Piaget (cognitive development), Erikson (psychosocial development), Maslow (hierarchy of needs)
- **Relational and self-recognition theories**: Bowlby (attachment theory), Rochat (model of self-awareness)

In addition, Luyckx (dual-cycle model of identity development) and Wilber (integral theory) were evaluated. Each theory was analyzed from multiple independent perspectives to provide a multi-angled account.

The evaluation was conducted through analysis of each theory's structural descriptions based on primary literature and examination of their correspondences with the stages of the five-stage model. The strength of correspondence was classified into two categories: theories in which a clear structural correspondence was confirmed, and theories in which a partial or conditional correspondence was confirmed.

## 3. Overview of the Five-Stage Model

As a premise necessary for understanding the findings, the five-stage model is defined here.

The five-stage model is a hypothesis that describes a structure common to processes in which something is generated. It concerns the creation of existence itself, from physical phenomena to psychological phenomena. It consists of the following five stages.

**Field (場)**: an undifferentiated state. Neither direction nor structure has yet been determined. Nothing has yet occurred, and no discomfort is present. It is the matrix of possibility, but that possibility is not yet visible to anyone.

**Wave (波)**: a stage in which multiple directions diverge and compete. Differences emerge within the Field, and opposition arises. Signals appear that something is wrong, but the direction of movement is not yet fixed.

**Edge (縁)**: a tense state in which opposing elements coexist without converging on either side. Contradictions intensify most strongly here, and relations intersect in a way that cannot be contained by simple resolution. Whether one can remain at this stage determines the quality of what is generated.

**Vortex (渦)**: the stage in which a new coherence spontaneously emerges from within tension. Multiple elements draw one another in and produce a self-sustaining dynamic. It is not yet a finished form, but an explicable shape begins to appear.

**Bundle (束)**: the stage in which form becomes fixed and stabilizes as a reusable structure. What has been generated acquires a certain directionality and becomes able to function as a premise for other processes. However, Bundle is not an endpoint; it is also the point of return from which the next Field begins.

These five stages are cyclical rather than linear. The return from Bundle to Field, that is, the beginning of a new undifferentiated state on the basis of an already generated structure, is what allows generation to continue.

If these definitions already produce a sense of discomfort, that itself is an important perspective for this investigation. Whether the five-stage model is valid is precisely the question this investigation seeks to test. It is entirely acceptable to continue reading while retaining that discomfort.

## 4. Findings

### Overall Picture

Of the eleven theories examined, six showed clear structural correspondence, and five showed partial or conditional correspondence. No theory was found to lack structural correspondence altogether.

Structural developmental theories (Kegan, Cook-Greuter) exhibited more direct stage correspondence than theories from any other area. The sociocultural approach (Vygotsky) and the dynamic systems approach each make distinctive contributions, particularly regarding Edge and mechanism. Classical stage theories (Piaget, Erikson) are especially valuable as descriptions of differences from the five-stage model.

### Kegan's Structural Developmental Theory

Robert Kegan (born 1946, Harvard Graduate School of Education) describes human development in terms of five "orders of consciousness." Each order is defined by the boundary between subject and object. What is "subject" at one order, that is, what one is embedded in and cannot reflect upon, becomes "object" at the next order, that is, something that can be made the object of reflection. This transformation from subject to object is the common mechanism underlying every stage transition.

This series of transformations corresponds to the five-stage model at the functional level. Orders 0-1 (incorporative and impulsive) correspond to the undifferentiated state in which one is embedded in perception and impulse (Field). Order 2 (instrumental) corresponds to the stage in which one's own needs and interests become differentiated and arise as conflict (Wave). Order 3 (interpersonal) corresponds to the stage in which the self is constituted through reciprocity with others (Edge). Order 4 (institutional) corresponds to the stage in which identity is established as a coherent whole (Vortex). Order 5 (interindividual) corresponds to the stage in which multiple self-systems interpenetrate and are integrated (Bundle).

Order 3 is especially noteworthy as a developmental-psychological realization of Edge. At Order 3, the self is constituted within a web of relations with others, and the contour of the self is produced by the mesh of those relations. Which relations define the self is not determined in advance, and this embeddedness in relationship functions as the precondition for Order 4 (Vortex).

The repeated structure in which "what had been subject becomes object" is a distinctive description of mechanism not present in the five-stage model itself. The five-stage model does not explicitly describe the mechanism of transition between stages, but Kegan's formulation, according to which what had hitherto been subject becomes object at the next stage, can be read as one account of that transition mechanism.

Moreover, the transition from Order 3 to Order 4 can be read as a developmental-psychological description of the transition from Edge to Vortex. At Order 3, the self is embedded in relationships (an Edge-like condition); at Order 4, a self-authoring identity is established as a coherent whole (a Vortex-like condition). The fact that this transition requires a long-term process lasting several years to over a decade corresponds structurally to the five-stage model's claim that movement from Edge to Vortex does not occur immediately, and that the time one remains at Edge bears on the quality of what is generated.

### Rochat's Model of Self-Awareness

Philippe Rochat (born 1953, Emory University) proposed a five-level model describing how self-awareness emerges in infancy and early childhood. The model captures the process by which self-awareness unfolds sequentially from birth to around age five, positioning responses to mirror images, including the mark test, as developmental indicators. The five levels are as follows. With Level 0 ("Confusion": undifferentiation of self and world) as a contrastive baseline, development proceeds through Level 1 ("Differentiation"), Level 2 ("Situation"), Level 3 ("Identification"), Level 4 ("Permanence"), and Level 5 ("Self-consciousness/Meta self-awareness").

This sequence describes the movement from Field to Wave to Edge to Vortex as an ontogenetic unfolding of self-awareness. Level 0 is an undifferentiated condition in which self and world are fused, and it corresponds directly to the definition of Field. At Level 1, separation between one's own kinesthetic sense and the external world begins to emerge (Wave). At Level 2, the self begins to be positioned within the environment (Edge). At Level 3, a coherent "I" is identified (Vortex). The moment one looks in a mirror and recognizes "that is me" is a powerful observable indicator of Vortex, the emergence of the individual.

There is room for further examination regarding the treatment of Level 4 (Permanence) and Level 5 (Meta self-awareness) together as Bundle. These are qualitatively different capacities. Level 4 corresponds to the aspect of remaining as structure, whereas Level 5 may be read more naturally as re-entry into the next Field, namely, a new horizon opened by the perspective of others.

It is highly suggestive that Rochat himself speaks of developmental levels as "attractors" in a dynamic system. Stages are understood not as fixed endpoints but as transitions between stable states. In addition, Rochat's own suggestion that in new social situations, such as entering preschool or transferring schools, the same pattern of undifferentiation, differentiation, relation, and self-identification may recur resonates directly with the fractal character of the five stages.

### Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development

Lev Vygotsky (1896-1934, Moscow University) argued that development arises not from an individual's internal maturation but through interaction within social, cultural, and historical contexts. His central concept, the "Zone of Proximal Development" (ZPD), refers to the domain of development in which a child cannot succeed alone but can do so with the support of a more capable other. The educational practice most widely understood as realizing the ZPD is "scaffolding," the process of adjusting support to the learner's capacity and gradually removing it.

The ZPD shows a strong correspondence as a sociocultural description of Edge. This zone lies at the boundary between known and unknown structures, and can be read precisely as the educational realization of Edge, a tense condition in which opposing elements coexist. The ZPD exists only within the relation between learner and more capable other. It is neither what the learner can do alone nor mere imitation under total support. The fact that capability is extended in and through relation structurally overlaps with the essential features of Edge.

Vygotsky's central concept of the internalization of social interaction is also one description of the mechanism of transition from Edge to Vortex. What arises in relation, socially shared knowledge and skill, gradually becomes established as an individual's internal structure. This process of internalization is structurally homologous with Kegan's subject-to-object transformation and Bowlby's formation of internal working models.

### Dynamic Systems Approach

Esther Thelen (1941-2004) and Linda B. Smith (Indiana University) constructed a theoretical framework that describes development not as continuous linear progression but as the self-organization of a multivariable system. Within this approach, patterns of behavior appear and disappear as phase transitions accompanying changes in control parameters. Development is described as a process in which a stable attractor, that is, a behavioral pattern, becomes destabilized and shifts to a new attractor.

This framework provides a metatheoretical account of the five stages. A current attractor state (Field) is destabilized by changes in control parameters (Wave), and at a bifurcation point the old and new patterns coexist and compete (Edge). From within that condition, a new attractor emerges through self-organization (Vortex) and is stabilized through repeated use (Bundle).

The vicinity of a phase transition shows an especially strong correspondence as the dynamical-systems realization of Edge. At the bifurcation point, multiple variables, including body, environment, task, and memory, interact; old and new patterns coexist and compete; and which pattern will prevail is not determined in advance. The phase transition itself directly generates the emergence of a new attractor (Vortex).

The infant stepping reflex is a representative empirical example. The phenomenon in which the stepping reflex observed in the neonatal period disappears temporarily and later reappears as walking is explained not by neural maturation but by a phase transition produced by changes in the ratio of body weight to muscular strength, that is, a control parameter. This sequence of destabilization, criticality, and the emergence of new order is a dynamic realization of Field, Wave, Edge, and Vortex.

An important contribution of the dynamic systems approach is that it can describe in the vocabulary of dynamical systems the mechanisms of stage transition described by other developmental theories. Piagetian stage transition and Kegan's transformation of orders can both be described within this framework as phase transitions between attractors.

### Bowlby's Attachment Theory

John Bowlby (1907-1990, Tavistock Institute of Human Relations) described the attachment bond formed between infant and caregiver and the mechanism through which this bond shapes interpersonal relationships across the lifespan. His key concept, the "secure base," refers to the stable relation provided by the caregiver, and it is because such a secure base exists that the infant can explore an unknown environment. Attachment patterns are classified as secure, avoidant, anxious/ambivalent, and disorganized, and are measured through Ainsworth's Strange Situation procedure.

Attachment theory describes the most primordial realization of the cycle Field to Wave to Edge to Vortex to Bundle to Field. From the secure base (Field), exploration begins (Wave), encounter with the unknown occurs (Edge), experience is integrated (Vortex), and the infant returns to the secure base where the internal working model is stabilized (Bundle to Field). The stable repetition of this cycle constitutes predictive models of self and other, namely, internal working models.

This theory makes a distinctive contribution to understanding Field. Field is not mere emptiness; it is a space actively sustained by a safe and supportive relation. It is therefore inaccurate to read Field as a state in which there is simply nothing. The image of "an oceanic condition in which everything is dissolved together" also implies the possibility of "remaining safely dissolved."

### Piaget's Cognitive Development

Jean Piaget (1896-1980, University of Geneva) described cognitive development as proceeding through four stages: sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, and formal operational. Its core mechanisms are threefold: assimilation, the incorporation of new experience into existing schemas; accommodation, the modification of schemas in response to new experience; and equilibration, movement to a higher-order equilibrium through the balancing of assimilation and accommodation. Piaget's constructivism, according to which knowledge is not passively received but actively constructed by the subject through interaction with the environment, resonates with the five-stage claim that creation does not occur passively but emerges from interaction within the Field.

The correspondence with the five-stage model is clear at the level of stage structure: existing schema (Field), encounter with information that cannot be assimilated (Wave), revision at the interface between schema and experience (Edge), establishment of a new schema (Vortex), and stabilization through equilibration (Bundle). However, there is a decisive difference here.

Equilibration is a process that seeks to resolve discrepancy and move toward stability. By contrast, Edge in the five-stage model places value on remaining with contradiction without resolving it. This difference is structurally important and is discussed in detail in Section 5, "Cross-Cutting Insights."

### Erikson's Psychosocial Development

Erik H. Erikson (1902-1994, Harvard University) described human development as unfolding through eight psychosocial stages across the lifespan. Each stage has its own distinctive "crisis," a conflict between two opposing tendencies: basic trust versus mistrust (infancy), autonomy versus shame and doubt (early childhood), initiative versus guilt (play age), industry versus inferiority (school age), identity versus role confusion (adolescence), intimacy versus isolation (young adulthood), generativity versus stagnation (adulthood), and integrity versus despair (old age). Resolution of the crisis at each stage leads to the acquisition of a "virtue."

It is difficult to map the eight stages directly onto the five stages, and any such attempt would be forced. Rather, each Eriksonian stage should be read as containing its own five-stage cycle. For example, in Stage 5 (identity versus role confusion), one can describe a cycle consisting of the plurality of social roles in adolescence (Field), fluctuations of identity and role experimentation (Wave), commitment decisions (Edge), establishment of identity (Vortex), and acquisition of fidelity (Bundle). Erikson's own epigenetic principle, according to which each stage may be reactivated across the lifespan, resonates structurally with the return from Bundle to Field.

Like Piaget, Erikson's crises move toward resolution. The value that the five-stage model places on remaining at Edge is not explicitly described within Erikson's framework. However, the fact that "foreclosure," premature fixation of identity without sufficient exploration, is regarded as problematic is noteworthy. It suggests, from the reverse side, the value of remaining at Edge.

### Cook-Greuter's Ego Development Theory

Susanne Cook-Greuter (Harvard Graduate School of Education) extended Loevinger's (1976) ego development theory, especially by offering a more refined description of development beyond the autonomous stage. The principal stages unfold as impulsive, self-protective, conformist, conscientious, individualistic, autonomous, construct-aware, and unitive. Measurement is conducted using the Washington University Sentence Completion Test (SCT), and approximately 80 percent of adults are located at the conformist to conscientious stages.

Structural correspondence with the five-stage model is second only to Kegan. Both theories belong to the same lineage of structural development and share a transformational sequence of undifferentiation, differentiation, relation, integration, and the transcendence of integration. The correspondence can be stated as follows: impulsive (Field), self-protective to conformist (Wave), conscientious to individualistic (Edge), autonomous (Vortex), and construct-aware to unitive (Bundle).

Cook-Greuter's distinctive contribution lies in its refined description of post-autonomous stages. The construct-aware stage is the stage at which ego structure itself is recognized as a construct, and this deeply resonates with the self-referentiality implied by Bundle, namely, structure recognizing itself as structure. The unitive stage can be read as a conscious return to Field. What Kegan describes in terms of subject-object transformation, Cook-Greuter describes as a shift in the center of gravity of the ego. Both illuminate the same structural development with different vocabularies, but Cook-Greuter provides a more refined description of what lies beyond, especially the deconstruction of ego at the construct-aware stage.

However, there is an empirical limitation: those who reach these higher stages are few in number, and sample sizes are therefore limited.

### Other Theories (Conditional Correspondence)

**Luyckx's dual-cycle model (DIDS)**: Koen Luyckx (KU Leuven) decomposed identity formation into subprocesses of exploration and commitment, and described a dual-cycle structure in which a formation cycle (broad exploration to commitment making) and an evaluation cycle (deep exploration to identification with commitment) operate repeatedly. Correspondence with the five stages should not be read as linear stage correspondence, but as correspondence in the form of a recurrent cycle. This model points to the limitations of reading the five stages only as stages, because the five dimensions may operate simultaneously, making a linear interpretation such as "Wave comes after Field, and Edge comes after Wave" inaccurate. Moreover, ruminative exploration, the state of remaining lost among alternatives and failing to reach commitment formation, can be read as a pathological form of remaining in Wave.

**Maslow's hierarchy of needs**: Abraham Maslow (1908-1970, Brandeis University) described human needs as hierarchically organized into five stages: physiological needs, safety, belonging and love, esteem, and self-actualization. Structural correspondence with the five-stage model is limited. The similarity in stage number is merely superficial. The content of the needs (physiological, safety, belonging, esteem, self-actualization) and the structure of the five stages (undifferentiation, fluctuation, boundary, individual emergence, stabilization) differ qualitatively. The former is a hierarchy of contents, whereas the latter is a process structure.

**Wilber's integral theory (AQAL)**: Ken Wilber (born 1949, Integral Institute) describes AQAL ("All Quadrants, All Levels") as a metatheoretical framework for integratively describing human experience and development. Its value lies less in direct correspondence with the five stages than in functioning as a checking framework. AQAL's four quadrants (interior-individual, exterior-individual, interior-collective, exterior-collective) can serve as a tool for determining in which quadrant a five-stage description is located. At the same time, the falsifiability of AQAL, the fact that its ability to correspond to virtually anything is both its theoretical strength and its weakness, also serves as a warning that applies to the five-stage model.

## 5. Cross-Cutting Insights

### The Difference Between Equilibration and Error Retention

The dominant paradigms of developmental psychology, such as Piaget's equilibration and Erikson's resolution of crisis, describe movement in the direction of resolving discrepancy and attaining stability. By contrast, Edge in the five-stage model locates value in retaining discrepancy as a question rather than immediately eliminating it. This divergence in direction is not superficial; it is structurally important.

Developmental psychology contains almost no concept equivalent to retaining discrepancy. This very absence serves as a mirror that illuminates the distinctiveness of the five-stage model. At the same time, it remains unresolved whether equilibration and the retention of discrepancy are wholly opposed. If retaining discrepancy is simply what it means to stop partway through equilibration, then the two may differ not by opposition but by phase.

### Field as a Safe Relational Space

Bowlby's secure base, Vygotsky's social relational field, and Kegan's embedded condition at Order 0 all show that Field is not an empty void but a space within which one can safely remain dissolved. For Field to be constituted, a safe relation is required as a precondition. This is an important finding that connects across the findings from other domains as well.

### Internalization as the Mechanism of Transition from Edge to Vortex

Vygotsky's internalization of social interaction, Bowlby's formation of internal working models, and Kegan's subject-to-object transformation are all different descriptions of the mechanism by which what arises at Edge, within relation, takes shape as Vortex, a coherence of the self. This process, in which what emerged in relation becomes part of the self, namely internalization, is a strong candidate for the mechanism of the five stages.

### Support for Fractality

Theories of developmental psychology strongly support the fractality of the five stages, namely the possibility that the same structure recurs at each scale. Kegan identifies an isomorphic transformation of subject to object within each stage transition, Rochat suggests recurrence in new social situations, and the dynamic systems approach describes isomorphic transitions between attractors at the scales of movement, cognition, and development. Similar structures in attachment patterns have also been reported across dyadic relations, families, and organizations.

## 6. Unresolved Questions

The following questions remain unanswered in this investigation.

**The structural relation between equilibration and retention of discrepancy**: Are equilibration, that is, discrepancy resolution, and the retention of discrepancy opposed, or are they different phases of the same process? If retaining discrepancy means stopping midway through equilibration, then they are not opposites. This question remains unresolved.

**Are the five stages "stages" or a "cycle"?**: Luyckx's dual-cycle model and the dynamic systems approach suggest that the five stages may be read not as a linear stage progression but as a cycle or attractor. How to integrate stage-like and cyclical characteristics remains unresolved.

**The empirical basis of post-autonomous stages**: Kegan's Order 5 and Cook-Greuter's higher stages are the most useful materials for describing the return from Bundle to Field, but those who attain these stages are few and sample sizes are limited.

**The relation between attachment patterns and discrepancy detection**: It is theoretically plausible that attachment patterns (secure, avoidant, anxious, disorganized) influence the capacity to detect or retain discrepancy, but there are no empirical data.

**The falsifiability of metatheory**: Both Wilber's integral theory and the five-stage model possess a structure that can be made to correspond to virtually anything. If correspondences can be found in every domain, is that a strength of the theory or a weakness? This question needs to be examined together with the findings from complexity science (D29).

## 7. Summary

Investigation of eleven theories in developmental psychology confirmed clear structural correspondence in six cases and conditional correspondence in five. Structural developmental theories in particular, namely Kegan and Cook-Greuter, showed more direct stage correspondence than theories in any other domain.

The degree of confidence in the findings can be stated as follows. The existence of structural correspondence can be regarded as confirmed. In particular, Kegan's subject-to-object transformation offers a strong candidate for the mechanism of stage transition in the five-stage model, and Vygotsky's internalization is promising as a mechanism describing the transition from Edge to Vortex.

At the same time, the fact that the dominant paradigms of developmental psychology describe discrepancy resolution whereas the five-stage model places value on remaining with contradiction rather than resolving it is not merely a correspondence but a significant difference. This difference illuminates the distinctiveness of the five-stage model while also raising the unresolved question of the relation between equilibration and the retention of discrepancy.

The insight suggested by Bowlby's secure base, that Field is a safe relational space, together with the fractality of the five stages broadly supported by developmental psychology, constitutes an important finding that connects across the findings from other domains.
