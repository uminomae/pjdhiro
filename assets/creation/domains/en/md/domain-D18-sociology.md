## 1. Purpose and Question of the Investigation

![Sociology: Weak Ties Generate Structure — 調査概要インフォグラフィック](https://uminomae.github.io/pjdhiro/assets/creation/img/svg/domains/ja/D18-01-overview-svg.svg)


This report presents the results of an investigation into the following question, using major theories in sociology.

> Do the theories of this academic domain correspond structurally to the five-stage model, Field -> Wave -> Edge -> Vortex -> Bundle?

Sociology is a discipline centered on the transition from interaction to institutionalization. How do individual actions generate social structure? How does social structure constrain individual action while also making it possible? Why do institutions arise, why do they become similar, and why do they collapse? The purpose of this investigation is to examine how the structure of the five-stage model corresponds to the existing theoretical systems that address these questions.

This document is a report of findings. It is neither a sociology textbook nor a proof that the five-stage model is correct. Places where structural correspondence was found and places where it was not are reported with equal weight.

## 2. Method of Investigation

In this investigation, eleven theories were selected from sociology.

**Selection criteria**: The set was composed to cover three evaluation lenses.

- the relation between action and structure, including recursivity, duality, and dialectic
- mechanisms by which social order is generated, including institutionalization, legitimation, and norm formation
- micro-macro linkage, from individual action to collective pattern to structure

The set includes major lineages of sociology, from classical theory such as Durkheim and Weber to modern theory such as Luhmann and Collins.

**Method of evaluation**: For each theory, structural correspondence with the five-stage model was examined from multiple independent angles, and the strength of correspondence was then judged.

**Criteria of judgment**:

- **Strong structural correspondence**: The central concepts and processes of the theory show clear structural correspondence with stages of the five-stage model.
- **Partial correspondence**: Some stages correspond, but the overall correspondence remains limited.
- **Conditional correspondence**: Correspondence is possible, but the theory remains too abstract for concrete validation through specific cases.

**Limits of the investigation**: This investigation examines structural correspondence rather than causal relation. It is also based on major works rather than exhaustive examination of each theorist's full corpus. Independent review remains incomplete, so later verification may alter some of the evaluations.

## 3. Overview of the Model

The five-stage model used in this investigation describes a common structure of creative process through five stages.

**Field**: An undifferentiated condition. It is the initial state in which neither direction nor structure has yet been fixed.

**Wave**: A stage of exploration in which multiple directions diverge and compete. Difference becomes visible and fluctuation appears.

**Edge**: A tense condition in which opposing elements coexist without converging into either side. Whether a process can remain at this stage bears on the quality of the structure that later emerges.

**Vortex**: A stage in which a new coherence or order rises spontaneously from within the tension. It is generated from inside rather than imposed from outside.

**Bundle**: A stage in which form is fixed and stabilized as a reusable structure. Bundle becomes the condition for the next Field, and the cycle continues.

The model is not restricted to a single scale, whether individual, organization, or whole society. It rests on the hypothesis that the same structure may appear in face-to-face interaction, in the formation of institutions, and in social change at large.

If these definitions already feel uncomfortable, that discomfort is itself an important point for the investigation. Readers may continue while retaining it.

## 5. Results: Findings by Area

### The Value of Weak Ties: Granovetter

In his 1973 article *The Strength of Weak Ties*, Granovetter analyzed the relation between tie strength and information flow in social networks. Close friends, strong ties, tend to share the same information, producing high redundancy. By contrast, acquaintances seen only occasionally, weak ties, bridge otherwise separate clusters and bring in genuinely new information. Originally grounded in research on job search, this finding has since been confirmed across fields such as innovation, social capital, and public health.

The correspondence with the five-stage model is clear. Dense clusters held together by strong ties correspond to Field as closed informational space. Detection of new information or opportunity corresponds to Wave. The bridging function of weak ties between clusters is one of the purest sociological formulations of Edge, because the weak tie is itself the bridge connecting different Fields. Formation of new patterns through circulation of information, resources, and influence corresponds to Vortex. Stabilization of a new network structure corresponds to Bundle.

The paradox here, that incomplete relation rather than complete relation brings novelty, is one of the strongest findings in the whole investigation concerning Edge.

### The Power of Face-to-Face Encounter: Collins's Interaction Ritual Chains

Extending Goffman's work on interaction order, Collins argues that chains of interaction rituals in face-to-face situations generate social structure. A successful ritual requires four elements: bodily co-presence, boundary against outsiders, common focus of attention, and shared mood leading to mutual emotional entrainment. When successful, the ritual generates the state Durkheim called collective effervescence, and emotional energy accumulates in individuals as motivation for later encounters.

The five-stage correspondence is as follows. Face-to-face co-presence corresponds to Field as the bodily precondition. Convergence toward a common focus corresponds to Wave. Rhythmic synchronization and emotional resonance among participants correspond to Edge. Collective effervescence as emotional peak corresponds to Vortex. Accumulated emotional energy and the production of collective symbols correspond to Bundle, which is then carried into the next interaction. The repeated chain is itself a Bundle -> Field cycle.

Where many other theories describe institutional or societal macro-levels, Collins gives a full-cycle account at the micro-level of face-to-face interaction. It is therefore one of the clearest examples of the five-stage process operating at the smallest social scale.

### Is Structure Constraint or Condition of Possibility? Giddens's Structuration Theory

Giddens responds to the traditional opposition between structure and agency through the concept of the duality of structure. Structure constrains action, but at the same time makes action possible. Structure is not external to action. It is reproduced continuously through action itself. Actors reflexively monitor both their own conduct and that of others, and possess knowledge of the conditions and consequences of action.

In relation to the five-stage model, this theory gives one of the clearest formulations of the Bundle -> Field cycle. Rules and resources as latent structure correspond to Field, because they condition action. Difference and conflict emerging in action situations correspond to Wave. The deployment of rules and organization of relation in interaction correspond to Edge. Repeated practice rising into institutional pattern corresponds to Vortex. Reproduced institutions guiding the conditions of later action correspond to Bundle, which then returns as the next Field.

Giddens offers an important insight for the five-stage model: Bundle is both constraint and enabling condition. Existing institutions, habits, and knowledge limit the next act of creation, but without them the next act of creation cannot begin.

### How Reality Is Constructed: Berger and Luckmann

Berger and Luckmann argue that society consists in a dialectical cycle of externalization, objectivation, and internalization. Actors project meaning outward, repeated actions solidify into habitualized institutions, and through socialization those institutions are internalized into the subjectivity of the next generation. Their core formulation is that society is a human product, society is an objective reality, and human beings are a social product.

In correspondence with the five-stage model, the taken-for-granted reality of the everyday world corresponds to Field. Projection of meaning through externalization corresponds to Wave. Institutionalization as reciprocal typification of habitualized action corresponds to Edge. Self-explanation of institutional order through legitimation corresponds to Vortex. Internalization into subjectivity through socialization, followed by renewed externalization, corresponds to Bundle returning to Field.

A major reservation is necessary. Berger and Luckmann explicitly state that these three moments are analytically distinct but operate simultaneously in social process. This means the five stages must be read here not as rigid temporal sequence but as phases of transformation.

### Why Organizations Become Similar: DiMaggio and Powell's Institutional Isomorphism

DiMaggio and Powell ask why organizations become homogeneous. They identify three mechanisms of isomorphism: coercive isomorphism through political influence and regulation, mimetic isomorphism through imitation under uncertainty, and normative isomorphism through professionalization and shared values.

In terms of the five-stage model, an unstructured population of organizations corresponds to Field. Pressure of legitimacy and uncertainty correspond to Wave. Most important here is the process they call structuration of the organizational field, involving increased interaction, emergence of domination and coalition structures, increase of information load, and development of mutual awareness. This is a particularly concrete description of how Edge is composed step by step. Synergistic action of the three isomorphic mechanisms in producing an institutional type corresponds to Vortex. Reproduction of isomorphic practices and standards corresponds to Bundle.

This theory also has a distinctive implication: institutional isomorphism describes movement from diversity toward homogeneity, which can look like the reverse of the usual creative path from undifferentiated condition toward articulated structure. It therefore clarifies one mechanism by which Bundle constrains diversity.

## 6. Cross-Domain Patterns

![Sociology: Weak Ties Generate Structure — 横断的パターン図](https://uminomae.github.io/pjdhiro/assets/creation/img/svg/domains/ja/D18-03-cross-patterns-svg.svg)


### Diversity of Edge Implementations

All eleven theories show a different implementation of Edge. In Granovetter, Edge appears as bridging between clusters. In Collins, as emotional synchronization. In Giddens, as deployment of rules in interaction. In Rogers, as diffusion along networks. In DiMaggio and Powell, as field structuration. In Berger and Luckmann, as reciprocal typification. In social movement theory, as mobilization structure. In Bourdieu, as competition over position in a field. In Durkheim, as interdependence through division of labor. In Weber, as the organization of relation between charisma and followers. In Luhmann, as structural coupling between systems.

This diversity suggests that Edge is not defined by one concrete form, but by the structural feature of tense coexistence among opposed elements. Which aspect of social relation is isolated will determine how Edge appears.

### Cross-Scale Pattern from Micro to Macro

Collins works at the micro-level of face-to-face interaction, Granovetter at the meso-level of network structure, and Durkheim and Weber at the macro-level of society as a whole. Whether the same pattern truly repeats across scales remains a hypothesis, but sociology is one of the domains that suggests that possibility from multiple theoretical angles.

### Explicit Formulation of the Bundle -> Field Cycle

Giddens's duality of structure, Berger and Luckmann's externalization -> objectivation -> internalization, Collins's interaction ritual chains, and cyclical understandings of contention in social movement theory all describe mechanisms by which Bundle, stable structure, becomes the Field conditioning the next round of action and creation. Sociology is one of the domains in which the Bundle -> Field cycle has been independently formulated most often.

## 7. Unresolved Questions

- **The conceptual difference between Bourdieu's field and Field in the five-stage model**: Bourdieu's champ is already a structured competitive space and differs conceptually from Field as undifferentiated possibility space. Because of the risk of confusion through same-name different-meaning, careful distinction is necessary.
- **The relation between simultaneity and staged sequence**: Berger and Luckmann explicitly state that their three moments operate simultaneously in social process. This raises the methodological question of whether the stages should be read as strict temporal order or as phases of transformation.
- **The relation between isomorphism and creation**: Institutional isomorphism describes a movement from diversity toward sameness, which may appear opposite to the creative process usually described by the five-stage model. Whether the model's scope should include mechanisms of anti-creation remains unresolved.
- **The relation between emotional energy and trust**: Collins's emotional energy is produced by successful face-to-face ritual. Whether it also forms part of the affective basis of trust, and therefore part of the mechanism by which trust operates as social binding force, remains to be tested.
- **The ambiguity of anomie and Field**: Durkheimian anomie suggests that Field may be not only a space of possibility but also a source of instability when structure is weakened or collapses. How to position both sides of Field remains an open question.

## 8. Conclusion

Sociology is a domain with a high degree of structural correspondence to the five-stage model. Of the eleven theories examined, ten showed structural correspondence and one showed conditional correspondence.

Three findings are especially important.

First is the paradox shown by Granovetter's strength of weak ties: not complete relation, but incomplete relation, brings novelty. Together with the linguistic finding that inability to transmit everything can generate structure, this suggests independently across domains the creative value of incompleteness.

Second is the fact that eleven theories yielded eleven distinct types of Edge. Edge is therefore not a concept tied to one fixed form, but one defined by the structural feature of tense coexistence among opposed elements, and sociology is one of the richest domains for concrete variation in that structure.

Third is that the Bundle -> Field cycle is independently formulated in multiple theories, especially Giddens, Berger and Luckmann, Collins, and social movement theory. Giddens's insight that Bundle is both constraint and enabling condition is especially important for understanding the cyclical nature of the five-stage model.

These findings suggest that the structure of the five-stage model corresponds to several major sociological theories. At the same time, the question of whether the correspondence is one of structural identity or only of descriptive similarity remains open. Issues such as the terminological overlap with Bourdieu's field, Berger and Luckmann's simultaneity, and the high abstraction of Luhmann are not simply limits of the report. They are also starting points for further theoretical inquiry.
