# Business Management

## 1. Purpose of the Investigation

This report was prepared to answer the following question:

> Do the theories in this academic domain correspond structurally to the five-stage model (Field, Wave, Edge, Vortex, Bundle)?

It evaluates practical theories of organization, management, innovation, and design in terms of their structural correspondence to the five stages of creation. This is the densest domain for the present project (maximum 20 items; approved by pjdhiro on March 2, 2026).

This report presents the findings of the investigation. It is not a document written to justify any particular theory. Areas of strong correspondence and areas requiring reservation are described with equal care.

## 2. Method of Investigation

### Scope and Selection Criteria

In this domain, 11 theories and practical models were selected for evaluation. Selection was based on three criteria: (1) the theory explicitly addresses processes of generation or transformation; (2) structural analysis is possible on the basis of primary literature or well-established secondary literature; and (3) different lineages within the domain can be compared.

### Evaluation Method

For each theory, the investigation first explains what the theory describes, and then examines where correspondences to the five stages can be identified. Judgments of correspondence were made by considering together the theory's textual concepts, concrete examples, connections between stages, the presence or absence of circulation from Bundle to the next Field, and the risk of overinterpretation.

### Criteria of Judgment

- **Strong structural correspondence**: the core concepts of the theory correspond concretely to multiple stages of the five-stage model, and the reason this can be said is explainable in the main text
- **Conditional correspondence**: correspondence can be indicated, but interpretive reservations remain in the stage mapping
- **No correspondence confirmed**: there are partial approximations, but the theory cannot be read naturally as exhibiting the structure of the five stages as a whole

### Limits of the Investigation

This investigation does not provide an exhaustive examination of every work associated with each theory. It focuses on the central concepts of major works and extracts structure at a level of granularity that permits comparison. As a result, some details internal to the theories and some historiographical disputes within the scholarship are not treated exhaustively.

## 3. Overview of the Model

The five-stage model describes the process of creation through the following five stages.

| Stage | Definition |
|------|------|
| **Field (場)** | An undifferentiated state. It refers to an initial condition in which neither direction nor structure has yet been fixed. |
| **Wave (波)** | A stage of exploration in which multiple directions diverge and differences are amplified. |
| **Edge (縁)** | A stage of tension in which opposing elements coexist without immediately converging on either side. |
| **Vortex (渦)** | A stage in which a new coherence or order emerges out of tension. |
| **Bundle (束)** | A stage in which form becomes fixed and stabilizes as a reusable structure. |

This model is not a one-way line. It includes a circulation in which Bundle becomes the condition for the next Field. In addition, the five stages are not rigidly separated boxes but are better understood as a continuous spectrum. The model includes the hypothesis that the same structure can recur at different scales, from momentary thought to institutional formation and long-term historical change.

## 5. Findings: Examination of Major Theories

### 5.1 Nonaka & Takeuchi Organizational Knowledge Creation 5 Phases + SECI/Ba

The "Nonaka & Takeuchi Organizational Knowledge Creation 5 Phases + SECI/Ba" developed by Nonaka & Takeuchi (1995) and Nonaka, Umemoto & Senoo (1996) describes organizational knowledge creation in five phases: sharing tacit knowledge, creating concepts, justifying concepts, building an archetype, and cross-leveling knowledge (Nonaka et al. 1996). The SECI model presents a separate axis of description consisting of four modes of interaction between tacit and explicit knowledge: socialization, externalization, combination, and internalization.

The overall direction of the five phases is structurally aligned with the five-stage model. Sharing tacit knowledge corresponds to a shared space in which knowledge not yet verbalized is dissolved together (Field). Creating concepts corresponds to the emergence of differences and words from tacit mutual understanding (Wave). Justifying concepts corresponds to the stage in which concepts are related to external criteria, such as organizational intention and social consistency, and boundaries of acceptance and rejection are woven (Edge). Building an archetype corresponds to the emergence of an implementable coherence (Vortex). Cross-leveling corresponds to the diffusion of knowledge into other units, where it remains as structure and initiates the next cycle (Bundle). Since the SECI model is a different descriptive axis, the five phases correspond more directly to the overall five-stage process.

This is an organizational theory originating in Japan. It introduced Polanyi's concept of tacit knowledge into management studies and criticized the Western bias toward explicit knowledge. It stands, together with Weick's sensemaking and Senge's learning organization, as one of the three major pillars of organizational knowledge theory.

### 5.2 d.school Design Thinking 5 Modes

The "d.school Design Thinking 5 Modes" proposed by the Hasso Plattner Institute of Design (d.school) and Brown (2008) consists of the five modes Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test. The official description explicitly presents the process as nonlinear and iterative. Prototyping is framed as "Build to think," that is, iterative generation through which one approaches the question by making trial forms.

In this theory, correspondence with the five stages can be observed through the movement by which preconditions are formed, differences are amplified, relations are arranged, a new coherence arises, and that coherence becomes established.

This approach emerged from Stanford d.school and was widely disseminated in business through IDEO and Tim Brown. It has also been subject to substantial criticism for formalization and superficial adoption. The fact that the present project's title is "プロジェクトデザイン論" itself indicates the depth of its relation to Design Thinking.

### 5.3 Tuckman's 5 Stages of Small Group Development

The "Tuckman 5 Stages of Small Group Development" proposed by Tuckman (1965) and Tuckman & Jensen (1977) began with Tuckman's 1965 review of 50 studies on stages of small-group development, from which he proposed four stages: Forming, Storming, Norming, and Performing. In 1977, Adjourning was added, and the model became widely known as a five-stage sequence.

Forming corresponds to the emergence of the group's Field, in which purposes, roles, and boundaries are explored. Storming corresponds to Wave, where differences such as conflict of opinion and emotional friction surface. Norming corresponds to Edge, where roles, norms, and trust are organized and rules of relation are formed. Performing corresponds to Vortex, where outcomes are produced through functional division of roles. Adjourning is dissolution, but it also connects the inheritance of experience and achievements to the next organizational structure, and therefore corresponds to Bundle. Among the theories in D22, the correspondence of the first four stages is among the most intuitive and faithful.

This model emerged from small-group research in the 1960s. Its origin as a review article is distinctive. It has been widely cited in management, education, sports psychology, and related fields. Gersick's punctuated equilibrium model is an important alternative.

### 5.4 Christensen's Disruptive Innovation

Christensen's "Disruptive Innovation" (1997) argues that products initially inferior in performance enter from the low-end or from new markets and eventually replace existing markets. "The Innovator's Dilemma" names the paradox that firms doing the "right" thing nevertheless fail.

An existing value network corresponds to the stable operation of an existing paradigm (Field). The appearance of a disruptive technology corresponds to a weak signal (Wave). Trial activity in the low-end segment remains at the boundary between existing and new markets (Edge). The rise of the new market corresponds to self-reinforcing growth (Vortex). Completion of market transition corresponds to the establishment of a new industrial structure (Bundle). The correspondence to the five stages as a whole is clear.

The structure of the dilemma is its greatest distinctive feature. "Correct management" produces failure because organizations are embedded in value networks, that is, in worldviews, and reject weak signals, namely disruptive technologies, as mere error. This is the most robust organizational failure case of the stance that treats error as a question rather than discarding it. It is also an organizational version of the failure to retain tension: firms cannot hold the signal that "the market is still small" and continue allocating resources to the existing large market. This theory emerged from Harvard Business School, and its starting point was empirical research on the disk-drive industry. It became widely influential, though it has also faced criticism regarding empirical verification.

### 5.5 Senge's Learning Organization + Bion's Psychoanalytic Organizational Theory

The "Learning Organization + Bion's Psychoanalytic Organizational Theory" associated with Senge (1990), Argyris & Schön (1978), and Bion (1961) centers on five disciplines: systems thinking, personal mastery, mental models, shared vision, and team learning (Senge 1990). "Creative tension" refers to the condition in which the gap between vision and reality is not prematurely eliminated but retained as the driving force of learning.

Systems thinking corresponds to grasping the whole (Field). Creative tension corresponds to the gap between vision and reality (Wave). Dialogue corresponds to the meeting of different perspectives (Edge). Shared vision corresponds to the establishment of collective directionality (Vortex to Bundle). In a psychoanalytic reading, baD, baF, and baP describe three patterns in which the five-stage movement stops at Wave and cannot advance to Edge.

This work emerged from MIT Sloan and from the application of systems dynamics and psychoanalysis to organizational theory. Bohm's theory of dialogue describes the retention of collective tension as suspension of judgment.

### 5.6 March's Exploration/Exploitation + Ambidextrous Management

The "March Exploration/Exploitation + Ambidextrous Management" associated with March (1991) and O'Reilly & Tushman (2004) defines exploration as the pursuit of new possibilities and exploitation as the refinement of what is already known (March 1991). Excessive exploitation leads to a competency trap. Excessive exploration leads to exploration that never learns.

Exploration corresponds approximately to the first half of the five stages, from Field through Edge: openness to the unknown, fluctuation, and connection. Exploitation corresponds approximately to the second half, from Vortex through the refinement of Bundle. A competency trap is a condition of fixation in Bundle, in which an organization cannot return to Field. The garbage can model provides a management-theoretical description of Field, where structure arises contingently from undifferentiated flows.

The tradeoff between exploration and exploitation is the problem of where in the five stages an organization remains stuck. The existing recursive-function model EV-BZ-010 is important here: exploration corresponds to non-tail recursion, where the stack equals the retention of tension, whereas exploitation corresponds to iteration, which does not require a stack. A competency trap can therefore be read as fixation on tail-call optimization, that is, the disappearance of the stack, which means the disappearance of retained tension. This theory belongs to the Carnegie school of organization theory, in the lineage from Simon to March. Ambidextrous management became widely disseminated through HBR.

### 5.7 Weick's Sensemaking + HRO

The "Weick Sensemaking + HRO" associated with Weick (1995) and Weick & Sutcliffe (2007) describes sensemaking as the social process by which meaning is given to experience. Its seven properties are identity-grounded, retrospective, enactive of sensible environments, social, ongoing, focused on and by extracted cues, and driven by plausibility rather than accuracy (Weick 1995). Enactment means that action "creates" the environment. Observer and environment are inseparable.

The entire process of sensemaking corresponds structurally to the five stages. Raw cues drift in an undifferentiated state (Field). Awareness of discordance and weak signals corresponds to Wave. Enactment, the mutual generation of action and environment, constitutes Edge as a relational web in which subject and object are not cleanly separated. The clustering of a plausible story corresponds to Vortex. Settlement into an institutionalized system of meaning corresponds to Bundle. Enactment is especially one of the most sociologically precise descriptions of Edge. Its bidirectionality, in which actors create environments and environments constrain actors, satisfies the two defining conditions of Edge: a web of relations and indeterminacy.

1. **Priority of plausibility**: The five stages do not describe arrival at a single correct answer. Vortex is a provisional structure, and the criterion of judgment is not accuracy but actionability. This is isomorphic with the core of sensemaking.
2. **Retrospection**: Meaning is constructed after action. The direction from Field to Bundle advances temporally, but meaning-making is retrospective. This is an important supplement for explaining the nonlinear character of the five stages.

This work belongs to social psychology and organization theory. Weick stands in the lineage of social constructionism. Pushing beyond March and Simon's bounded rationality, he argues that "rationality" itself is retrospectively constituted. HRO has been empirically studied in high-risk industries such as nuclear power, aviation, and medicine. It also connects deeply with Bohmian dialogue in the Senge discussion and with March's concept of exploration.

### 5.8 TPS/Lean + Agile

The "TPS/Lean + Agile" associated with Ohno (1978), Womack & Jones (1996), and Ries (2011) begins with Ohno Taichi's two pillars of the Toyota Production System: JIT (Just-in-Time) and jidoka. Jidoka refers to automatic stopping when an abnormality is detected. The andon line-stop system institutionalizes this practice.

The full process of TPS and Agile corresponds to the five stages. Going to the actual place and seeing the actual thing corresponds to bodily immersion in the workplace prior to verbalization (Field). Abnormality detection and andon activation correspond to the manifestation of difference, while the Five Whys correspond to recursive deepening into Wave. Mixed-model production and sprint reviews correspond, albeit implicitly, to Edge as boundary contact among heterogeneous elements. MVPs and prototypes correspond to Vortex as provisional structures prior to completion. Standardized work and the institutionalization of kaizen correspond to Bundle as the structural settlement of learning. The Build-Measure-Learn cycle of Lean Startup can be read as a reverse-rotation loop from Vortex to Wave to Field, a high-speed version of the spiral return from Bundle to Field.

2. **The Five Whys as an amplifier of collective alpha function**: It is a procedure that repeatedly converts beta into alpha at the organizational level. It structures individual sensemaking and raises it into organizational knowledge.
4. **The sprint as a fractal micro-cycle from Field to Bundle**: A two-week sprint compresses and repeats the entire five-stage structure. This is a clear demonstration of fractality, in which the same structure appears at a short temporal scale.

### 5.9 Effectuation (Sarasvathy)

Sarasvathy's "Effectuation" (2001, 2008) describes the decision logic of expert entrepreneurs and contrasts it with causal logic (Sarasvathy 2001). Its five principles are Bird-in-Hand, Affordable Loss, Crazy Quilt, Lemonade, and Pilot-in-the-Plane.

The process of effectuation corresponds with high precision to the first half of the five stages. Bird-in-Hand corresponds to an undifferentiated condition in which one remains immersed in currently available means, self, knowledge, and networks, without a predefined goal (Field). Lemonade corresponds to detecting unexpected events not as problems but as resources (Wave, in an entrepreneurial register). Crazy Quilt corresponds to the emergence of purpose from the voluntary commitments of stakeholders not selected in advance (Edge). The clustering of a new purpose corresponds to Vortex. Transition to causal logic and institutionalization of the business correspond to Bundle. Crazy Quilt is especially one of the most refined entrepreneurial descriptions of Edge. Partners are not chosen in advance; purposes are determined in relation, and that relational web drives the Vortex, namely the business model.

1. **The contrast between causal logic and effectuation as a theoretical description of fixation in Bundle**: Causal logic, goal to means to execution, belongs to the activity of Bundle. Effectuation, means to relation to emergent purpose, belongs to the activity of Field through Edge. The bias of mature firms toward causal logic is isomorphic with the competency trap discussed in March.
2. **Lemonade as the most direct management-theoretical expression of treating prediction error as a resource**: The stance of not discarding prediction error but recovering it as a resource appears here in its entrepreneurial form.

This theory was developed at Carnegie Mellon University, in the lineage from Simon to March to Sarasvathy. It is grounded in Knight's (1921) concept of uncertainty and criticizes the overemphasis on planned strategy in management studies. It concretizes March's concept of exploration as entrepreneurial decision-making and also connects deeply with Weick's enactment.

### 5.10 Taleb's Antifragile + Black Swan

Taleb's "Antifragile + Black Swan" (2007, 2012, 2018) proposes a threefold classification of systems: fragile, robust, and antifragile, where antifragile systems strengthen under stress (Taleb 2012). A black swan is an event that is unpredictable, has extreme impact, and appears retrospectively "explainable" (Taleb 2007).

The overall structure of antifragility corresponds to the five stages. The operation of a stable state corresponds to the equilibrium aspect of Field. The arrival of a stressor or black swan corresponds to Wave, in which the predictive model fails fundamentally. Skin in the game corresponds to Edge as a boundary in bodily contact with the stressor. Overcompensation corresponds to Vortex, the clustering of a new structure that exceeds the prior one. The Lindy effect corresponds to Bundle as the stabilization of a structure tested by time. However, whereas the five-stage model describes a process of generation, Taleb describes a system property. Antifragility concerns a system's response characteristics under stress, not creation understood directly as process. This difference in descriptive level should not be confused.

2. **Optionality as the mathematical structure of retained tension**: The core of optionality is an asymmetric payoff structure. Loss is limited, while gain is theoretically unbounded. This grounds the economic value of retaining tension in option theory. Retaining tension corresponds to holding an option; action corresponds to exercising it.
3. **The barbell strategy as simultaneous retention of the two poles of Field and Bundle**: By excluding middling ordinary risk, one combines extreme stability with extreme openness. This is a radical expression of ambidextrous management. It reformulates March's exploration/exploitation tradeoff as the exclusion of the middle.

Taleb is a Lebanese trader and mathematician whose theory-building is rooted in practical experience as an options trader. His intellectual sources include Seneca and Montaigne. He is a fundamental critic of academic economics and financial engineering. His work is closely connected to March's exploration/exploitation, Sarasvathy's Affordable Loss, and real options theory. It can also be situated in the lineage of Knightian fundamental uncertainty within economics.

### 5.11 Scharmer's Theory U

Scharmer's "Theory U" (2007, 2009), further developed with Kaufer (2013), describes the transformation of the quality of the social field through a U-shaped process (Scharmer 2007). The three major movements of the U are: sensing, presencing, and realizing.

The seven stages of the U correspond structurally to the five stages. Downloading corresponds to the automatic operation of existing patterns, that is, the inertial repetition of Bundle before transition to Field. Seeing corresponds to the first germ of Wave, in which one suspends existing frameworks and sees facts. Sensing corresponds to a deepening of Wave, feeling the whole system from the boundary. Presencing corresponds to the deepest reach of Field, connection to the deepest source in an undifferentiated state. Crystallizing corresponds to the shift from Edge to Vortex, where the germ of structure arises from relation. Prototyping corresponds to Vortex as rapid experimental embodiment. Performing corresponds to Bundle as the institutionalization of new practice. The "descent" of the U, from Downloading to Presencing, is a movement of regression and deepening from Bundle to Field. The "ascent," from Presencing to Performing, is the movement of generation from Field to Bundle. The direction from Field to Bundle in the five-stage model corresponds to the right half of the U, while the left half describes the path required to arrive at Field, namely the relinquishing of existing patterns.

1. **Presencing as the bottom of Field**: The five-stage model presents Field as a point of departure, whereas Scharmer describes in detail the process of arriving at Field, from Downloading through Seeing and Sensing to Presencing. There are stages involved in "descending into Field." This supplements the question that the five-stage model leaves implicit: how one enters Field.
3. **The four levels of listening and their reverse-order correspondence to four layers**: Downloading (L3: existing behavior), factual listening (L1: detection of facts), empathic listening (L2-O: empathy), and generative listening (L0: embodied emergence). The deepening of listening from L3 to L0 is a movement from the surface to the depth of consciousness and describes the four-layer model from the side of lived awareness.

## 6. Cross-Cutting Patterns

First, as shown by Nonaka & Takeuchi Organizational Knowledge Creation 5 Phases + SECI/Ba, d.school Design Thinking 5 Modes, and Tuckman's 5 Stages of Small Group Development, this domain describes difference and conflict not merely as obstacles but as conditions under which new order arises. This means that within the five stages, the transition from Edge to Vortex is especially richly observable in this domain.

Second, Christensen's Disruptive Innovation, Senge's Learning Organization + Bion's Psychoanalytic Organizational Theory, and March's Exploration/Exploitation + Ambidextrous Management all share the view that generation is not a one-off event but a circulation including the formation of preconditions, the amplification of tension, local ordering, settlement, and subsequent reconfiguration. This confirms that the five-stage model must be read not as a static classification chart but as a temporal movement.

More important still is the fact that even when the correspondence rate is high, the theories are not all saying the same thing. One theory describes the conditions of Field in depth, another provides a detailed account of the tension of Edge, and yet another clearly shows the fixation of Bundle and the next circulation. This difference itself constitutes the thickness of the domain.

## 7. Unresolved Questions

1. Is the correspondence found in each theory an immanent stage structure of the theory itself, or a structure extracted by the analyst?

2. To what extent can the structure confirmed in this domain be extended from the explanation of individual cases to a general model of generation? Further examination is needed regarding the conditions under which comparability can be maintained without losing the specificity of the object.

3. Should the five stages be read as a linear progression, or as a circulation that includes multiple temporal layers and repetitions? In particular, theories differ in the degree to which they explicitly describe the return from Bundle to the next Field.

4. What becomes visible, and what is lost, when the domain-specific vocabulary of this field is translated into the general vocabulary of the five stages? The boundary between translatability and untranslatability is itself an important issue for future comparative investigation.

## 8. Conclusion

In this reconsideration of the domain, strong structural correspondence was confirmed in 11 of the 11 theories examined, and conditional correspondence in 0. In particular, Nonaka & Takeuchi Organizational Knowledge Creation 5 Phases + SECI/Ba, d.school Design Thinking 5 Modes, and Tuckman's 5 Stages of Small Group Development provide especially thick descriptions either of the movement from Field to Edge or of the transition from Edge to Vortex, and can therefore be positioned as core evidence within this domain.

At the same time, the findings of this domain do not justify the claim that exactly the same five stages appear in every theory without remainder. The stages that stand out most strongly differ from theory to theory, and in some cases the stage mapping still requires reservation. What matters, therefore, is not to generalize directly from the high correspondence rate, but to continue comparing which moment is described in which vocabulary.

In that sense, this domain does not simply support the validity of the five-stage model in a single direction; it is also a domain that imposes conditions on how the model must be read. The very coexistence of theories that correspond densely and theories that require reservation constitutes part of the value of the comparative investigation.
