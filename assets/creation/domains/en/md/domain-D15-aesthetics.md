# Aesthetics

## 1. Purpose and Question of the Investigation

![Aesthetics — 調査概要インフォグラフィック](https://uminomae.github.io/pjdhiro/assets/creation/img/svg/domains/ja/D15-01-overview-svg.svg)

This report was prepared to answer the following question.

> Do the theories of this academic domain correspond structurally to the five-stage model, Field -> Wave -> Edge -> Vortex -> Bundle?

The investigation evaluates theories that describe aesthetic experience as a process of generation by examining their structural correspondence with the five stages. Theories were drawn from four lineages: psychoanalytic aesthetics, Japanese aesthetics, Western philosophical aesthetics, and media aesthetics.

This document is a report of findings. It is not a document for justifying any particular theory. Places where correspondence is strong and places where reservation is still necessary are described with the same level of restraint.

## 2. Method of Investigation

### Objects and Selection Criteria

Ten theories or practical models were evaluated in this domain. Selection was guided by three criteria: (1) the theory explicitly treats processes of generation or transformation; (2) structural analysis is possible on the basis of primary texts or well-established secondary literature; and (3) it allows comparison across distinct lineages within the domain.

### Method of Evaluation

For each theory, the investigation first clarifies what the theory is describing and then examines which parts of the five-stage model it may correspond to. Judgments were made on the basis of concepts used in the texts, concrete examples, connection between stages, presence or absence of circulation from Bundle to the next Field, and the risk of overreading.

### Criteria of Judgment

- **Strong structural correspondence**: The core concepts of the theory correspond concretely to multiple stages of the five-stage model, and the report can explain why.
- **Conditional correspondence**: Correspondence can be indicated, but interpretive reservation remains in the stage mapping.
- **No correspondence confirmed**: Partial approximation is present, but the theory cannot be read naturally as a five-stage structure as a whole.

### Limits of the Investigation

This investigation does not survey the full corpus of each theory exhaustively. It focuses on the core concepts of major works and extracts structure at a level fine enough for comparison. That means some internal detail and some scholarly disagreement remain outside the present scope.

## 3. Overview of the Model

The five-stage model describes creative process through the following five stages.

| Stage | Definition |
|------|------|
| **Field** | An undifferentiated state, an initial condition in which neither direction nor structure has yet been fixed |
| **Wave** | A stage of exploration in which multiple directions diverge and difference is amplified |
| **Edge** | A stage of tension in which opposing elements coexist without immediately converging into one side |
| **Vortex** | A stage in which a new coherence or order rises out of the tension |
| **Bundle** | A stage in which form is fixed and stabilizes as a reusable structure |

This model is not a one-way straight line. It includes circulation in which Bundle becomes the condition for the next Field. The five stages are also better understood as a spectrum than as rigid boxes. The model includes the hypothesis that the same structure may recur at different scales, from momentary thought to institutional formation and long historical change.

If discomfort remains at this point, that is itself an important part of the investigation. Readers can continue while holding onto that discomfort.

All ten cases showed strong structural correspondence. None remained merely conditional and none failed to show correspondence. Taken as a whole, this domain shows a comparatively high density of correspondence with the five-stage model, though not all theories correspond in the same way. Some describe the conditions of Field in thick detail; others describe the tension of Edge or the fixation of Bundle with particular precision.

What matters most is not the raw correspondence rate, but which theory describes which stage in which vocabulary. In this domain, body, institution, time, perception, practice, and symbol illuminate different moments of the five stages.

## 5. Results: Examination of Major Theories

### 5.1 Meltzer's Aesthetic Conflict

Meltzer's aesthetic conflict describes aesthetic conflict as the tension between the external beauty of an object and the unknowability of its inner world. Two poles are active at once: curiosity and fear. Tolerance of that conflict is treated as a key condition of psychic development.

Encounter with the aesthetic object corresponds to Field. Simultaneous activation of the two poles corresponds to Wave. Retention of the conflict, or retention of tension, corresponds to Edge. The formation of aesthetic experience corresponds to Vortex. Creative expression corresponds to Bundle. All five stages are present, and the retention of tension as Edge stands at the center of the theory.

This is the only entry in D15 that describes aesthetic experience through a psychoanalytic framework. The unknowability of the other's inner world is the source of conflict, which places the first-person and second-person dimension at the center in a way not found in natural-scientific accounts. In contrast to the cognitive approaches of D14, this theory treats tolerance for what cannot be known as a condition of creativity. It depends on Kleinian object-relations theory and on integration with Bion's concept of containment.

### 5.2 Japanese Aesthetics and the Structure of Retention

The present investigation reads several strands of Japanese aesthetics together through a shared structure of retention: Zeami's famous claim that if one keeps something hidden, flower arises; Motoori's sensitivity to pathos; Hisamatsu's discussion of incompleteness; and related lines of thought in Japanese aesthetic tradition.

Aesthetic tradition as a way, or do, corresponds to Field. Perception of lack or incompleteness corresponds to Wave. Retention, hiding, enduring, or withholding corresponds to Edge. The momentary emergence of flower corresponds to Vortex. Formal pattern or cultural form corresponds to Bundle. What is especially strong here is that retention of tension as Edge appears independently across several traditions.

At the same time, these concepts belong to different historical contexts. Treating them together under the heading Japanese aesthetics is itself interpretive and requires caution, especially in light of the influence of Okakura's outward-facing discourse in *The Book of Tea*.

### 5.3 Kant's *Critique of Judgment* (Beauty and the Sublime)

In Kant, aesthetic judgment is disinterested pleasure, a judgment that is not driven by utility or concept but by the form of the object itself. The beautiful involves the free play of imagination and understanding, while the sublime radicalizes the same structure at the point where imagination meets its limit.

Sensuous intuition corresponds to Field. Mismatch between imagination and understanding corresponds to Wave. Disinterestedness, suspension of interest, corresponds to Edge. Free play between imagination and understanding corresponds to Vortex. The universal claim of taste corresponds to Bundle. The sublime may be read as an extreme version of the same process: the limit of imagination intensifies Wave, and transition toward reason forces the passage through Edge into a different Vortex.

Kant matters because he is the systematic starting point of modern Western aesthetics. Schiller, Hegel, Adorno, and Heidegger all develop their positions in response to Kant. The textual basis is strong, but the interpretive range remains wide. The justification for reading the theory as a strong correspondence is that the suspension of interest functions as the retention of tension and satisfies the central conditions associated with Edge.

### 5.4 Heidegger's *Origin of the Work of Art* (The Strife of World and Earth)

Heidegger describes the work of art as setting up a world while at the same time setting forth the earth. The essence of the work lies in the strife between world and earth. Truth, understood as unconcealment, occurs through the tension between illumination and concealment.

Earth as self-secluding ground corresponds to Field. The activation of strife between world and earth corresponds to Wave. The rift, the point at which the strife takes form, corresponds to Edge. The occurrence of truth corresponds to Vortex. Fixation as work, or Gestalt, corresponds to Bundle.

Heidegger is the only entry in D15 that describes aesthetic creation at an ontological level. Beauty is not treated as a matter of judgment or psychology, but as an event of being. The concept of rift is one of the most fundamental descriptions of Edge found in the entire domain. The interpretive range is broad, but the promotion to strong correspondence rests on the fact that rift satisfies the central conditions of Edge: it joins world and earth, remains unresolved, and drives the emergence of truth.

### 5.5 Dewey's *Art as Experience* (Rhythm and Consummation)

Dewey describes aesthetic experience through the idea of an experience: material passes toward fulfillment and closes as consummation, while each part flows into the next and the whole carries a distinctive quality. The root of experience lies in interaction between organism and environment and includes phases of lack, movement toward the environment, and recovery of equilibrium.

Interaction between organism and environment corresponds to Field. Emergence of lack or disturbance corresponds to Wave. The relation between doing and undergoing corresponds to Edge. The transformation of resistance into higher-order equilibrium corresponds to Vortex. Consummation corresponds to Bundle.

This is one of the most natural correspondences in D15. Dewey explicitly describes aesthetic experience as a process, which distinguishes him from Kant's focus on conditions of judgment and Heidegger's focus on ontological event. The structure in which lack becomes the material of creation resonates directly with the broader error-driven logic. It is also highly fractal: the same rhythm repeats in production, reception, and education.

### 5.6 Benjamin's *Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction*

Benjamin's concept of aura refers to the unique phenomenon of distance, however near the object may be, tied to singularity and authenticity. Mechanical reproduction detaches the artwork from the sphere of tradition and weakens aura.

Tradition, ritual, and aura correspond to Field. Technical reproduction, movement, copying, acceleration, corresponds to Wave. Reorganization of the polarity between cult value and exhibition value corresponds to Edge. Reception through shock and distraction corresponds to Vortex. Reversal of artistic function, including politicization of art, corresponds to Bundle.

Benjamin is the only entry in D15 that describes structural transformation of aesthetic experience through technological conditions. Where most other entries concern individual aesthetic experience, Benjamin treats the social arrangement of the aesthetic. Aura is therefore a unique description of irreversible transformation at the level of Field. The same pattern also recurs fractally across print, photography, film, and digital media.

### 5.7 Ranciere's *Distribution of the Sensible*

Ranciere's central concept is the distribution of the sensible, the regime that allocates what is visible or invisible, sayable or unsayable, audible or inaudible in a shared world. Dissensus disturbs that given order and allows what had not been countable to appear.

The established sensible order, the police, corresponds to Field. Dissensus, as rupture in that order, corresponds to Wave. Reorganization of the boundary between visible and invisible, or of who counts, corresponds to Edge. The emergence of those who had not counted corresponds to Vortex, though only in a limited sense. A new regime of sensible distribution corresponds to Bundle.

Ranciere is the only entry in D15 that treats aesthetic experience primarily as political-aesthetic arrangement of a shared world rather than as inner experience or ontology of the work. It expands the meaning of Field from individual psychology toward social distribution.

### 5.8 Schiller's *Letters on the Aesthetic Education of Man*

Schiller describes two fundamental drives: the sense drive, tied to change and material, and the form drive, tied to law and permanence. The play drive integrates the two. Beauty is the object of what Schiller calls living form.

The aesthetic state as determinability corresponds to Field, a state in which nothing is fixed and everything remains possible. Conflict between the sense drive and the form drive corresponds to Wave. The play drive, which joins the two, corresponds to Edge. Living form, the dynamic emergence of beauty, corresponds to Vortex. Recovery of the human whole through aesthetic education corresponds to Bundle.

Schiller provides one of the clearest philosophical definitions of Edge in the domain. The play drive is a third drive arising in the between of the other two, which makes it a direct philosophical articulation of relation as Edge. His notion of determinability is also one of the most precise philosophical descriptions of Field: not emptiness, but openness to all possible determinations.

### 5.9 Adorno's *Aesthetic Theory* (Dialectic of Mimesis and Rationality)

Adorno treats art as constituted by dialectical tension between mimesis, the expressive and nonconceptual moment, and rationality, the formal and constructive moment. Truth content emerges through negative relation to social reality, not through affirmation but through negation.

Social reality as negatively given background corresponds to Field. Mimesis, the stirring of what cannot be fully captured by concept, corresponds to Wave. Dialectical tension between mimesis and rationality corresponds to Edge. Emergence of truth content through negation corresponds to Vortex. The riddle-character of the artwork, closed yet open, corresponds to Bundle.

Adorno is the only entry in D15 that makes negativity itself central to aesthetic essence. This differs from conflict in Meltzer, lack in Dewey, and incompleteness in Japanese aesthetics. His notion of riddle-character also gives a distinctive account of Bundle: not simple completion, but a structure that can be interpreted and yet never exhausted.

### 5.10 Eco's *The Open Work* (Active Completion by the Receiver and Indeterminacy)

Eco's open work is not a fully closed structure, but one that permits multiple realizations through active intervention by the receiver. Intentional ambiguity and indeterminacy become sources of aesthetic value.

A field of possibilities corresponds to Field. Intentional indeterminacy and ambiguity correspond to Wave. The receiver's act of selection, the point at which relation with the work is formed, corresponds to Edge. Emergence of a particular realization corresponds to Vortex. Maintenance of an open structure within a finite space of possibility corresponds to Bundle.

Eco is the only entry in D15 that treats reception itself as an essential moment of creation. It is not the producer but the receiver who forms Edge. The theory also offers one of the most direct descriptions of a Bundle that does not fully close. By remaining unfinished, the work makes creative participation by the receiver possible.

## 6. Cross-Domain Patterns

![Aesthetics — 横断的パターン図](https://uminomae.github.io/pjdhiro/assets/creation/img/svg/domains/ja/D15-03-cross-patterns-svg.svg)

First, as Meltzer's aesthetic conflict, Japanese aesthetics, and Kant all show, difference and opposition in this domain are not described merely as obstacles. They are described as conditions for the emergence of new order. This means that in this domain the transition from Edge to Vortex can be observed with unusual richness.

Second, Heidegger, Dewey, and Benjamin all show that generation is not a single isolated event. It includes formation of preconditions, amplification of tension, local ordering, stabilization, and later reconfiguration. This confirms that the five-stage model must be read not as a static classification table but as temporal movement.

It is equally important that even when correspondence rates are high, the theories are not all saying the same thing. Some describe the conditions of Field in detail, others describe the tension of Edge, and still others clarify the fixation of Bundle and the return to the next cycle. That very difference is part of what gives the domain its density.

## 7. Unresolved Questions

1. Are the correspondences identified here structures internal to the theories themselves, or structures extracted by the analyst?

2. How far can the structures confirmed in this domain be extended from explanation of particular cases to a more general model of generation without losing the specificity of the objects?

3. Should the five stages be read as linear progression, or as circulation including multiple temporal layers and repetition? The degree to which each theory explicitly marks return from Bundle to the next Field differs considerably.

4. What becomes visible, and what is lost, when a domain's own vocabulary is translated into the general vocabulary of the five stages? The boundary between translatability and untranslatability is itself a major issue for future comparative work.

## 8. Conclusion

In this re-examination of the domain, strong structural correspondence was confirmed in all ten theories. None remained conditional.

Among the most important pieces of evidence are Meltzer's aesthetic conflict, Japanese aesthetics and the structure of retention, and Kant's *Critique of Judgment*. These describe with particular density either the movement from Field to Edge or the transition from Edge to Vortex.

At the same time, the findings do not imply that the same five stages appear in exactly the same way in every theory. The stages that stand out most strongly differ from one theory to another, and the mapping itself remains more robust in some cases than in others. The domain therefore supports the five-stage model not by uniform repetition, but through a dense plurality of structurally related descriptions.
