## 1. Purpose and Question of the Investigation

![Mathematics: Structure Condenses at Boundaries and Criticality — 調査概要インフォグラフィック](https://uminomae.github.io/pjdhiro/assets/creation/img/svg/domains/ja/D01-01-overview-svg.svg)


This report summarizes the results of an investigation into whether a range of mathematical theories structurally correspond to the five-stage model, consisting of Field (場), Wave (波), Edge (縁), Vortex (渦), and Bundle (束).

The five-stage model is a theoretical framework that describes a common structure through which creative processes pass. This investigation has been conducted across 30 academic domains, and the present report addresses the mathematics domain (D01).

**This report addresses a single question.** Do mathematical theorems and structures structurally correspond to the five-stage model? If so, how strong is that correspondence?

This report neither proves nor refutes any particular theory. Its purpose is to report the structural correspondences discovered through investigation, together with their relative strength.

---

## 2. Method of Investigation

### Scope

From the domain of mathematics, 11 theories and structures were selected. The criterion for selection was that the theory should contain a process in which structure emerges from the local to the global. Care was taken to span multiple areas of mathematics, from pure mathematics (sheaf theory, higher categories, Galois theory, Hodge theory) to applied mathematics (bifurcation theory, singular perturbation) and computational mathematics (persistent homology).

### Evaluation Method

For each theory, we examined its structural correspondence with each stage of the five-stage model: Field, Wave, Edge, Vortex, and Bundle. These correspondences were evaluated from multiple independent perspectives rather than on the basis of a single viewpoint. More specifically, after confirming the mathematical content of each theory, we analyzed whether it contained elements that structurally correspond to each stage of the five-stage model, and we derived conclusions through a comparative assessment that integrated multiple lines of interpretation.

### Criteria for Assessment

- **Strong structural correspondence**: Elements corresponding to all five stages can be clearly identified, and transitions between stages are also present within the theory.
- **Partial correspondence**: Some stages have clear counterparts, but correspondence with other stages is weak or becomes arbitrary.
- **Conditional correspondence**: The correspondence can be sustained depending on interpretation, but it cannot be said that the mathematical structure itself directly corresponds.

### Limits of the Investigation

This investigation has the following limitations.

- The correspondence between mathematical structures and creative processes is a structural similarity (an analogy), not a mathematical proof.
- Mathematical theories do not possess consciousness or intention. Accordingly, the extent to which a correspondence between "a process of conscious creation" and "mathematical structure without consciousness" is meaningful remains an unresolved question.
- The selection of these 11 theories is not exhaustive; other mathematical theories could also have been considered.

## 3. Overview of the Model

The five-stage model describes the structure traversed by creative processes in terms of the following five stages.

| Stage | Definition |
|------|------|
| **Field** | An undifferentiated state. An initial condition in which neither direction nor structure has yet been determined |
| **Wave** | A stage of exploration in which multiple directions diverge and compete |
| **Edge** | A state of tension in which opposing elements coexist without converging on either side |
| **Vortex** | A stage in which a new coherence (order) spontaneously arises from within that tension |
| **Bundle** | A stage in which form becomes determinate and stabilizes as a reusable structure |

This model argues that creation does not proceed in a straight line. Rather, it has a structure in which undifferentiation (Field) gives way to exploration (Wave), then to unresolved tension (Edge), from which order emerges (Vortex), and finally stabilizes (Bundle).

If these definitions feel strange, that is itself an important perspective for the investigation. Whether the five-stage model truly describes the structure of creation is precisely what this investigation seeks to examine, and there is value in continuing to read while retaining that sense of unease.

## 5. Results of the Investigation: Domain-Specific Findings

Below, especially suggestive theories are taken up for closer discussion.

### 5.1 Morse Theory: Topology Changes at Critical Points

Morse theory describes the phenomenon that, as the value of a Morse function defined on a smooth manifold varies, the topology of the space changes qualitatively at the moment a critical point is crossed. At a critical point, the shape of the sublevel set, that is, the set of points whose function value is less than or equal to a given value, changes fundamentally. This change is described by the operation known as handle attachment, and the type of handle added, namely its index, quantitatively characterizes the nature of the critical point.

The correspondence with the five-stage model is as follows.

- **Field**: the smooth manifold itself (the whole before structure has differentiated)
- **Wave**: the gradient flow of the Morse function (an exploratory stage in which flows arise in multiple directions)
- **Edge**: critical points (the moment of topological transition, where the new and old topological types coexist and neither is yet fixed)
- **Vortex**: handle attachment (irreversible reorganization of topology, in which a new order emerges)
- **Bundle**: handle decomposition (the point at which the manifold's overall topological structure becomes determinate)

This theory is noteworthy as a correspondence with the five-stage model because all five stages have clear counterparts and the transitions between them are naturally included within the theory itself. In particular, the sequence Edge -> Vortex -> Bundle, in which topology becomes undecidable at a critical point, is reorganized through handle attachment, and finally settles into a determinate structure of the manifold, is the most direct realization of the latter half of the five-stage model.

It should also be noted that critical points in Morse theory carry the quantitative datum of an index, which is additional information absent from the five-stage model. The insight that the nature of a critical point (Edge) is not uniform, but is mathematically distinguished in terms of which directions of instability are present, may offer a clue for refining the model.

### 5.2 Sheaf Theory and Čech Cohomology: From the Local to the Global

A sheaf is a mathematical framework for managing, in a coherent way, data associated with each part of a space. When a space is divided into small regions (open sets), data such as functions or sections are assigned to each region. The sheaf condition is the property that, if the data agree on the overlaps of adjacent regions, they can be glued into a global datum. Čech cohomology is the mathematical tool that measures whether or not this gluing fails.

The correspondence with the five-stage model is as follows.

- **Field**: the underlying space (the whole space on which the data live)
- **Wave**: local data (fragmentary information assigned to each region)
- **Edge**: compatibility conditions on overlaps (the tension of whether data on neighboring regions agree)
- **Vortex**: nontrivial cocycles (twisted structures arising from compatibility conditions)
- **Bundle**: global objects (determinate structures such as fiber bundles)

The distinctive feature of this correspondence is that the very constructive sequence of sheaf theory, local -> overlap -> cocycle -> global structure, runs in parallel with the progression of the five-stage model. In particular, the compatibility conditions corresponding to Edge rigorously formalize the reweaving of relations and thus structurally correspond to the definition of Edge as a state in which opposing elements coexist without converging on either side.

### 5.3 Bifurcation Theory: Parameter Changes Produce Qualitative Transitions

Bifurcation theory is the field that studies phenomena in dynamical systems such as differential equations in which continuous changes in parameters produce qualitative changes in the number or stability of solutions. For example, in a saddle-node bifurcation, a stable equilibrium point and an unstable equilibrium point collide and disappear, while in a Hopf bifurcation a periodic oscillation (limit cycle) emerges from a stationary state.

The correspondence with the five-stage model is as follows.

- **Field**: parameter space (the entire space that determines the conditions of the system)
- **Wave**: perturbations and fluctuations (exploration brought about by continuous parameter change)
- **Edge**: bifurcation points (critical states in which multiple solutions coexist and the destination remains unresolved)
- **Vortex**: separation and reorganization of solutions (the process in which new stable states are formed after bifurcation)
- **Bundle**: new stable states (structures fixed as attractors)

Bifurcation theory is especially strong because it satisfies all three characteristics of Edge: the presence of a network of relations, the presence of indeterminacy, and a connection to Vortex. At a bifurcation point, multiple solutions coexist (network of relations), which direction the system will take depends on minute perturbations (indeterminacy), and new stable states are formed after bifurcation (connection to Vortex).

Moreover, bifurcation theory is applied across many domains, including physics, chemistry, ecology, and economics, and thus provides a cross-domain point of contact with other investigated domains, including D02 physics, D03 chemistry, and D29 complex systems science.

### 5.4 Singular Perturbation and Boundary Layers: The Essential Structure Resides in the Terms That Are Discarded

Singular perturbation is the field that studies phenomena in differential equations containing a small parameter for which the limit as that parameter approaches zero cannot be handled by ordinary methods and becomes singular. If one solves such an equation by the usual approximation method (regular approximation), one obtains a good approximation on the global scale, but the solution breaks down near the boundary. The narrow region in which this breakdown occurs is the boundary layer. Within the boundary layer, rapid change is condensed, and by connecting the inner and outer solutions through matching, one obtains a unified solution valid across the entire domain.

The correspondence with the five-stage model is as follows.

- **Field**: the state space of the system as a whole
- **Wave**: perturbation by a small parameter (the stage in which the outer solution governs the global domain)
- **Edge**: the boundary layer (the narrow region at the margin where structure condenses)
- **Vortex**: matching (the process of connecting the inner and outer solutions)
- **Bundle**: uniform asymptotic expansion (a unified solution valid over the entire domain)

What makes this theory stand out in the investigation is that it gives mathematically precise form to the insight that the essential structure resides in what is discarded. In regular approximation, small terms are discarded as negligible, yet those discarded terms are precisely what generate the decisive structure of the boundary layer. If they are discarded merely because they are small, one loses sight of the system's essential behavior.

It should also be noted that matching, the connection between the inner and outer solutions, is a reciprocal construction not present in the five-stage model and therefore differs from the one-way progression presupposed by that model.

## 6. Cross-Cutting Patterns

![Mathematics: Structure Condenses at Boundaries and Criticality — 横断的パターン図](https://uminomae.github.io/pjdhiro/assets/creation/img/svg/domains/ja/D01-03-cross-patterns-svg.svg)


Across the 11 theories, the following patterns repeatedly appeared.

### 6.1 Structure Condenses at Boundaries and Criticality

The boundary of a Julia set, the compatibility conditions of a sheaf, the link in a Milnor fibration, bifurcation points, boundary layers, and critical points in Morse theory: these six theories all exhibited the common pattern that the richest structure condenses at locations corresponding to the boundary or the critical region of a space or system. This structurally corresponds to the Edge stage of the five-stage model.

In mathematics, it is widely recognized that points in the interior tend to exhibit comparatively simple behavior, whereas points at the boundary or at criticality carry complexity and richness. The fact that this pattern concentrated so strongly around Edge in the present investigation suggests that the structure of the five-stage model aligns with mathematical intuition.

### 6.2 A Mixture of the Abstract-Coherence Type and the Critical-Trigger Type

The theories in the mathematical domain can be broadly divided into two types.

- **Abstract-coherence type**: global objects are constructed from the coherence of algebraic or category-theoretic structures (sheaf theory, higher categories, Galois theory, Hodge theory)
- **Critical-trigger type**: qualitative transitions occur at critical points as parameters or function values change (bifurcation theory, Morse theory)

Julia sets, Milnor fibrations, singular perturbation, and persistent homology are hybrid cases that combine both types. The theories with strong correspondence to the five-stage model tended to be these hybrid cases.

### 6.3 A Shared Weakness in Conditional Correspondence

The three theories judged to exhibit conditional correspondence, higher categories, Galois theory, and Hodge theory, share a common weakness. In these theories, the element corresponding to Edge is deterministic, and the indeterminacy required by the five-stage model for Edge, namely a tension that does not settle into either side, is weak. Commutative diagrams in higher category theory are determinate, and the correspondences of Galois theory are likewise fixed as one-to-one correspondences. This suggests that Edge in the five-stage model is not merely an intermediate stage, but includes indeterminacy as an essential condition.

## 7. Unresolved Questions

This investigation did not reach a conclusion on the following questions.

### Where Is the Boundary Between Structural Similarity and Metaphor?

The sheaf-theoretic procedure by which local data are assembled into fiber bundles runs directly in parallel with the five-stage model, but Galois theory contains the inclusion reversal relation, a structure absent from the five-stage model. At present, no established quantitative method exists for distinguishing between "structurally similar" and "similar only as metaphor."

### How Should the Absence of Consciousness Be Interpreted?

No mathematical theory possesses consciousness. The five-stage model describes creative processes and ordinarily presupposes a conscious subject. Correspondence with mathematical structure can therefore be interpreted in two ways: either as a description of creativity without consciousness, or as a description of the structural skeleton of creation itself.

### Are the Five Stages Discrete or Continuous?

The five-stage model is defined discretely as five stages, but persistent homology and bifurcation theory deal with continuous parameter change. The possibility that the five stages are in fact a convenient segmentation of a continuous spectrum deserves examination.

### Where Is the Edge in Spinors?

Interpretations differ regarding the correspondence of the five stages in the 720-degree rotation of spinors. One view takes the sign inversion at 360 degrees as Edge, while another treats the entire process of 720-degree rotation as Edge. Under the latter interpretation, the location of Vortex remains indeterminate.

## 8. Conclusion

The results of the investigation in the mathematics domain (D01) can be summarized as follows.

Of the 11 theories evaluated, 6 showed strong structural correspondence, 2 showed partial correspondence, and 3 showed conditional correspondence. There were no theories for which no correspondence was found.

The most reliable finding is that, **in many mathematical theories, structure condenses at locations corresponding to boundaries or criticality, and this structurally corresponds to the Edge stage of the five-stage model.** This finding was repeatedly confirmed across six independent theories: Julia sets, sheaf theory, Milnor fibrations, bifurcation theory, singular perturbation, and Morse theory.

One possible hypothesis is that the structure described by the five-stage model is not unique to human creative processes, but may function as a description of the more general process by which structure emerges from the local to the global. Mathematics is the discipline that describes this process in its most abstract form, and the broad presence of structural correspondence with the five-stage model supports that hypothesis.

That said, this correspondence remains a structural similarity, that is, an analogy rather than a mathematical proof. Because mathematical structures do not possess consciousness, further investigation is needed to determine how essential the correspondence between "the structure of the creative process" and "mathematical structure" truly is. The present level of confidence is best described as a strong indication, not a settled conclusion.
