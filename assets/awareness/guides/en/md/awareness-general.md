# What Is Awareness? — From Body, Prediction, Emotion, and Sociality to Intersubjectivity

## Purpose of This Document

This document is a map for exploring awareness. Rather than declaring a finished theory, it distinguishes between findings established in prior research and hypotheses currently under investigation.

The study of awareness is a vast field. This document focuses on four threads — body, prediction, emotion, and sociality — and traces a path toward one central question: how essential is intersubjectivity (mutual understanding with others) to awareness itself?

- Awareness is grounded in bodily signals. The brain operates as a system for processing predictions and prediction errors. Emotion arises from the integration of bodily states and context. These are findings established by prior research.

## Established Findings

### Body and Awareness

Our awareness does not live in the head alone. Heartbeat, breathing, visceral sensation — these bodily signals form the foundation of awareness.

Craig (2002) identified the afferent system that transmits information about the body's internal physiological state to the brain (interoception). This signal cannot be switched off. Closing your eyes blocks vision, but bodily sensation continues without interruption. This is the deepest layer of awareness.

Damasio (1994) demonstrated that bodily sensations accompanying emotion guide decision-making. Patients with damage to certain brain regions retain their intellectual capacities while experiencing severe difficulty with ordinary judgment. Bodily signals give experience its value and direction.

### The Predicting Brain

Friston (2010) formulated the brain's basic operating principle as the minimization of prediction error. The brain continuously maintains predictions about the environment and the body. When a discrepancy is detected between a prediction and actual input, the brain either updates the prediction or acts to reduce the error.

In everyday terms, this is the feeling that "something is off" or "something doesn't quite fit." Much of this mismatch is processed before it reaches awareness, but some surfaces as conscious experience.

### Where Does Emotion Come From?

The origin of emotion has been the subject of major ongoing debate.

Barrett (2017) argues that emotions are the brain's construction — a result of prediction, bodily signal, context, and concept working together. "Anger" and "sadness" are not pre-wired circuits but categories that are constructed anew each time.

Panksepp (1998), by contrast, argues that seven primary emotional systems are preserved through evolution.

This debate has been described as "irreconcilable but complementary" (van Heijst et al. 2025). Barrett explains how emotions are constructed; Panksepp explains why they exist. Crucially, both positions agree that emotion is deeply tied to the body and plays a role in directing action.

### The Social Brain

Beyond what we have seen so far — bodily signals, prediction and error, emotion — the social dimension is indispensable for understanding the structure of awareness.

Coan & Sbarra (2015) proposed Social Baseline Theory: the brain assumes social connection as its default. When social proximity is secured, threat responses are reduced. Isolation is a deviation from this baseline — a state that costs the brain additional resources.

Eisenberger et al. (2003) showed that social exclusion activates neural circuits that partially overlap with those involved in physical pain. The interpretation of this finding remains contested, but it is widely accepted that social signals are treated as "urgent" by the brain.

Large-scale epidemiological data support this direction. Holt-Lunstad et al. (2015), in a meta-analysis of 3.4 million people, showed that social isolation significantly increases mortality risk. Baumeister & Leary (1995) systematically demonstrated that the need to belong is a fundamental motivational force.

These findings show that social connection is not a "nice to have" but a factor directly linked to survival and health.

## The Question of Intersubjectivity

Looking at these findings together, a question emerges:

> What place do signals related to social connection occupy within awareness?

If bodily signals are the foundation of awareness, and the loss of social connection triggers responses comparable to physical threats, then how essential is intersubjectivity — mutual understanding with others — to awareness itself?

This exploration investigates the following hypothesis:

> Signals related to intersubjectivity are processed by the nervous system with roughly the same weight as signals related to survival.

This is not a claim that intersubjectivity ranks "second only to money" or is "useful to have." It is a view that it matters at a level equivalent to life itself. This is not a rejection of capitalism or economic rationality. Survival and intersubjectivity are distinguishable but not separable — an inclusive relationship, like the interplay of yin and yang.

This hypothesis is under investigation. Circumstantial evidence from multiple fields points in the same direction, but no single study has directly tested it.

## Premises and Reference Domains

This exploration draws on neurophenomenology and psychology as its core reference domains.

The work of Craig, Damasio, Friston, Barrett, and Panksepp forms the foundation as established findings from prior research. For circumstantial evidence bearing on the core hypothesis, this exploration references work by Coan, Eisenberger, Holt-Lunstad, Baumeister & Leary, Dunbar, and Cole.

Philosophical and clinical traditions — Merleau-Ponty's intercorporeality, Stolorow's intersubjectivity theory, Nagarjuna's dependent origination — are positioned not as "evidence" but as "different perspectives pointing in the same direction."

## Caveats and Open Questions

This exploration is ongoing. Please note the following.

**Relatively well-established**: That bodily signals form the foundation of awareness. That prediction and error processing is the brain's basic operating principle. That social isolation increases health risk.

**Hypotheses under investigation**: That intersubjectivity signals carry the same weight as survival signals. That survival and intersubjectivity constitute independent foundational dimensions.

**Next steps**: The path toward trust from intersubjectivity, and further toward the concept of containment, is the next stage of exploration.
