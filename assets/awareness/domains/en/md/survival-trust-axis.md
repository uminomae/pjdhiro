# Report on the Survival-Trust Axis

## 1. Purpose of This Document

This document explains a way of describing the direction in which tension, discomfort, or urgency is being interpreted within human experience.
It focuses on a framework that reads that direction through two axes: survival and trust.

## 2. Central View

The survival axis concerns orientations such as danger avoidance, securing safety, defense, and self-protection.
It includes not only bodily danger but also efforts to protect position or resources.

The trust axis concerns orientations such as relationship, belonging, trust, being understood, and intersubjectivity.
What matters here is that the trust axis is not treated as a simple classification of feeling, but as a way of reading whether connection with others is being maintained or threatened.

These two axes are not a chart for sorting emotion words.
They function instead as coordinates for asking what a person is trying to protect and what remains lacking or unresolved.

## 3. Why It Matters

The same event can lead to different emotional movements and different next actions depending on whether it is interpreted mainly as a matter of safety or mainly as a matter of relationship.

For example, if criticism is read as something dangerous that requires self-protection, defensive reaction is likely to move to the front.
If the same event is read as something that destabilizes relationship, efforts toward repair or confirmation may become more prominent.

Being able to distinguish that difference reduces the risk of misreading the underlying difficulty behind reactions that look similar on the surface.

## 4. What Can Be Said at Present

At present, the main advantage of separating the survival axis from the trust axis is that discomfort or tension no longer has to be treated as a single undifferentiated category.

In particular, the view that relationship-related problems can continue to feel unresolved when they are repeatedly processed as survival problems remains important.
Even if a person moves toward performance, defense, or superiority, a sense of lack may remain if the issue is relational rather than protective.

The trust axis also cannot yet be fixed to a single term.
Attachment, trust, belonging, and intersubjectivity overlap, but they are not identical.
At the moment, the least forced summary is to treat them more broadly as orientations related to intersubjective connection.

## 5. How Far It Is Settled

This two-axis frame is a strong working hypothesis, but it is not yet a final definition.
In particular, the trust axis still leaves open the question of which term should be central and which phenomena belong within the same field.

The survival axis also needs further refinement, especially on the question of how far it should include social defense and resource protection in addition to bodily danger.

## 6. Questions for Further Work

- Which term should be treated as the center of the trust axis?
- How far should social defense and resource protection be included in the survival axis?
- Are two axes sufficient, or is another axis required?
- How can these orientations be connected to observable forms of description?

## 7. Summary

The survival-trust axis is one of the most promising current ways of reading human experience at the level of minimal directionality.
Its strength lies not in listing emotion words, but in helping distinguish what a person is trying to protect and what remains lacking or unresolved.

At the same time, the framework remains under review.
The next task is to sharpen the contour of the trust axis and improve its observational tractability.
