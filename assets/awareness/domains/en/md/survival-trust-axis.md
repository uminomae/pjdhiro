# Report on the Survival-Trust Axis

## 1. Purpose and Guiding Question

This document is a report on how far human experience can be described through two directions of interpretation: survival and trust.

Its central question is what the survival-trust axis can explain as a core working hypothesis of the awareness model, and how far the trust axis can be sharpened after incorporating the developmental psychology intake.

## 3. Organizing the Survival-Trust Axis

The survival axis concerns orientations such as danger avoidance, securing safety, defense, and preservation of position or resources.
At present, it includes bodily threat clearly, while social defense and resource protection remain candidate extensions that still need refinement.

The trust axis is not treated as a mere category of pleasant feeling.
It is a direction for reading whether relationship is being maintained, whether connection remains viable, and whether one is situated in a repairable relational field.
Here, trust is not a fixed definition but one candidate within a broader cluster that includes attachment, belonging, trust, and intersubjectivity.

The two axes therefore function not as a table of emotion labels, but as coordinates for asking what a person is trying to protect and what remains lacking or unresolved.

## 4. Findings at Present

### 4-1. The trust axis has a developmental basis

`attachment-safe-base-reading.md` and
`developmental-psychology-intake-map.md`
indicate that attachment and secure base can be read as the developmental basis of the trust axis.

What matters is that safety is not treated as a vague feeling.
It is supported by repeated relational experience.
A secure base opens the possibility of exploration, and relational stability makes it possible for response not to collapse into immediate defense alone.

This suggests that the trust axis should be read less as a chosen value stance and more as an orientation supported by a history of relationship.

### 4-2. Relational safety is supported not only by co-presence but by internalized relational resources

`CN-001_internalized-relationship.md` and
the developmental psychology intake clarify that relational safety does not depend only on the presence of someone here and now.

Through repeated experience, a secure base can become internalized as an internal working model.
Because of that, even when another person is not physically present, internalized relational resources can still stabilize the trust axis.

This means the trust axis should not be reduced to immediate interpersonal preference.
It must also be read as a condition in which relational history has entered bodily expectation and behavioral interpretation.
One consequence is that relational safety should not be treated as a mere individual skill.

### 4-3. Repairability is a candidate route to observability

`trust-and-intersubjectivity-reading.md` and
`CN-007_iss42-measurement-design-principles.md`
point toward reading relational safety not as a condition in which nothing ever breaks, but as an expectation that rupture can be repaired.

Within that frame,
repair events,
repair success rate,
and repair latency become candidate proxy measures that move the trust axis closer to observable description.

That said, repairability is not treated here as the final definition of the trust axis itself.
At present it is better understood as a strong but limited observational candidate for relational safety.

### 4-4. The higher-order expression of the trust axis remains unsettled

When the developmental psychology intake is read together with
`trust-and-intersubjectivity-reading.md`,
trust appears as a strong candidate, but not as the trust axis itself.

Attachment provides developmental grounding.
Trust provides a central candidate term.
Intersubjectivity provides a wider higher-order expression.
For now, the least forced summary is to treat the trust axis broadly as an orientation related to intersubjective connection.

## 5. Limits and Falsifiability

At least four points remain unresolved.

First, the central term of the trust axis is not fixed.
It is still open whether the axis should lean most heavily toward trust, attachment, belonging, or intersubjectivity.

Second, the survival axis still requires refinement on how far social defense and resource protection should be included within it.

Third, relational safety remains theoretically unsettled.
It is not yet clear whether it should be treated simply as part of the trust axis or as a more independent condition within the model.

Fourth, developmental psychology strengthens the developmental basis of the trust axis, but it does not explain the awareness model as a whole.
Questions involving bodily state, foregrounding of discrepancy, and first-person descriptive units must still be judged alongside other research lines.

## 6. Conclusion

The survival-trust axis remains a strong current hypothesis for reading experience in terms of both what a person is trying to protect and which relational conditions have been lost or weakened.

What becomes clearer through the developmental psychology intake is that the trust axis has a developmental basis in attachment and secure base, can be sustained through internal working models, and may be connected to observability through repairability.

At the same time, the final central term of the trust axis, the exact status of relational safety, and the boundary with the survival axis all remain open.
