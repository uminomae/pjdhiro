# Report on the Survival-Trust Axis

## 1. Purpose of This Document

![Overview diagram of the Survival-Trust Axis](https://uminomae.github.io/pjdhiro/assets/awareness/img/svg/topics/en/survival-trust-axis-01-overview-svg.svg)

This document organizes the core hypothesis of the survival-trust axis and the circumstantial evidence that supports it.

The core hypothesis is as follows:

> Among the cognitive signals that ascend from the nervous system to consciousness, signals relating to intersubjectivity (trust) are processed with roughly the same weight as signals relating to survival.

This is not a claim that trust comes "second only to money," but rather the view that it matters at a level equivalent to life itself. It is not a denial of capitalism or economic rationality; rather, it is an attempt to position survival and intersubjectivity as co-equal foundational dimensions.

## 2. Definition of the Two Axes

The **survival axis** concerns the avoidance of danger, the securing of safety, self-defense, and the preservation of resources and standing. It encompasses not only physical threats but also social self-protection and self-preservation.

The **trust axis** concerns reading whether a relationship is being maintained, whether connection can continue, and whether one is in a situation where repair is possible. It is not merely a classification of pleasant emotions; it is treated as a candidate cluster spanning attachment, belonging, trust, and intersubjectivity.

These two axes are not a table listing emotion names. They are coordinates for distinguishing what a person is trying to protect and with what they are trying to maintain relationship.

The survival axis and the trust axis are conceived as independent dimensions, analogous to orthogonal basis vectors, but the details of this structure remain undetermined. As Nagarjuna's dependent origination suggests, they may ultimately stand in an inclusive relationship where distinction is possible but separation is not.

## 3. Multi-Level Circumstantial Evidence

The core hypothesis has not been directly verified by any single study. Circumstantial evidence spanning multiple levels of analysis points toward the same intersection from different angles — like the blind men and the elephant.

### Molecular Level

**Cole (2007)**: Differential expression of 209 genes was found in leukocytes of socially isolated individuals. A pathway by which the perception of social threat alters gene expression was identified. Subsequent research confirmed this across 11 conditions in cross-sectional studies. The initial study had N=14 and is correlational.

### Endocrine Level

**Smith & Wang (2014)**: Using an animal model, this study demonstrated that oxytocin causally mediates social buffering. However, extrapolation to humans faces reproducibility problems, and this line of evidence is assessed as weak.

### Neural Circuit Level

**Eisenberger et al. (2003)**: Social exclusion was found to activate neural circuits that partially overlap with those for physical pain. The current consensus is that these represent "distinct signals sharing, in part, the same emergency channel."

**Panksepp (1998)**: Identified a primary emotional system (the PANIC/GRIEF system) that responds to separation distress. This work demonstrates that the loss of social connection triggers a biologically grounded pain response.

### Physiological / Behavioral Level

**Coan & Sbarra (2015)**: Social Baseline Theory. The brain presupposes proximity to social relationships as its default, treating social resources as "bioenergetic resources equivalent to oxygen and glucose." Social Baseline Theory contains an even stronger claim than the core hypothesis presented here.

### Psychological Level

**Baumeister & Leary (1995)**: Met all nine criteria for designating the need to belong as a fundamental human motivation. Cited more than 30,000 times.

### Epidemiological Level

**Holt-Lunstad et al. (2010, 2015)**: A meta-analysis of up to 3.4 million individuals. Social isolation was shown to significantly increase mortality risk. These findings are probabilistic and exhibit variability across variables.

### Evolutionary Level

**Dunbar (1998)**: The social brain hypothesis. A correlation between neocortex ratio and social group size in primates. The field has since moved toward a multi-factor co-evolutionary model, but the contribution of social factors remains widely recognized.

### Social / Historical Level

**Durkheim (1897)**: Decline in social integration increases suicide rates. The core proposition has been sustained through 130 years of replication.

## 4. Evaluation of the Evidence

**Most robust**: The Holt-Lunstad meta-analyses (3.4 million individuals), Baumeister & Leary (systematic examination against nine criteria), and Durkheim's epidemiological accumulation.

**Circumstantial evidence for causal mechanisms**: Cole's CTRA (molecular pathway), Smith & Wang (oxytocin pathway, animal model), Coan's hand-holding experiment (changes in neural activity).

**Contested**: Interpretation of dACC activation, the Panksepp vs. Barrett debate on emotion categories, the precision of Dunbar's number, and the efficacy of oxytocin administration in humans.

**A survey of 30 fields found no research directly falsifying the core hypothesis.**

## 5. Developmental Foundations of the Trust Axis

Findings from developmental psychology provide concrete grounding for the trust axis.

**Attachment and the Secure Base**: A sense of security is not merely a mood; it is supported by repeated relational experiences. The presence of a secure base opens the possibility of exploration, enabling movement that is not confined to immediate defense.

**Internal Working Models**: Relational security does not rest solely on whoever is physically present; relational resources internalized through repeated experience support the stability of the trust axis.

**Repairability**: Relational security concerns not "never being damaged" but rather "the expectation that damage can be repaired." The occurrence of repair, its success rate, and its speed are candidate observables for the trust axis.

**Higher-Level Representations of the Trust Axis**: Trust is a strong candidate, but whether attachment (developmental foundation), trust (central candidate), or intersubjectivity (higher-level representation) most aptly anchors the axis remains undetermined. For now, framing it broadly as "the orientation concerned with intersubjective connection" is the most conservative arrangement.

## 6. Falsifiability

The core hypothesis would be rejected if any of the following were demonstrated:

- A cognitive signal stronger than both survival and trust (intersubjectivity) is confirmed to flow through the nervous system, taking priority over both.
- An informational category other than survival and trust is shown to be processed with the highest priority in human consciousness and the mind.

## 7. Limitations and Future Directions

- Whether the survival and trust axes are orthogonal independent dimensions or stand in a more complex relationship remains undetermined.
- The selection of the central term for the trust axis (trust, attachment, belonging, intersubjectivity) has not been finalized.
- Precision is needed regarding how far social self-defense is included within the survival axis.
- An operational description of non-duality (the inseparability of survival and intersubjectivity) is outstanding.
- Further deepening from intersubjectivity to trust to containment remains a task for future work.
